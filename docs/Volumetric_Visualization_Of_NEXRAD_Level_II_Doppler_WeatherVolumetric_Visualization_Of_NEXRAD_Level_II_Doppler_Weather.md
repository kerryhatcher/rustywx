## Abstract

Weather visualization is critical for operational forecasters and weather researchers to analyze, monitor, and predict severe weather events such as storms, tornadoes, and hurricanes. Usually measured weather datasets such as NEXRAD Level II radar data contain some errors and have temporal aliasing issues, so that the raw data cannot represent the true meteorological patterns. Existing weather visualization packages usually ignore these problems, thus they fail to unveil some detailed information and cannot meet the needs of professional meteorologists. Most displays generated by those packages are limited to 2D images, 3D point clouds, or iso-surfaces. Those displays are not able to accurately represent the details of the data. The limitation of visualizing data from a single radar site at one single time step in most packages blocks desirable information in the datasets.

We developed an efficient and accurate visualization tool capable of displaying the long-track 3D NEXRAD Level II volumetric weather data from multiple sites. The data were captured and integrated from three sites (KEAX, KILX and KSLX), and rendered by using the hardware-accelerated volume-rendering technique. Customized transfer functions are also provided for users to map data values to optical properties such as color and opacity to create different views. We also implemented a compression algorithm to compress 3D textures in order to largely reduce the consumption of hard disk space and to enhance the rendering speed without degrading image quality. An example study shows the tracking of a 24-hour supercell storm observed on March 12, 2006, in the Midwest of the United States. Images and animations from our implementation coupled with results from a set of experiments demonstrate that our methods and approaches are fast and robust, making them suitable for processing and rendering huge amounts of Doppler Level II weather data from multiple stations.

## Chapter 1: Introduction

Severe weather events cause significant injuries, fatalities, and property damage annually. In the United States, tornadoes average 1,500 injuries and 80 fatalities per year, thunderstorms result in 2,000 injuries and 150 deaths annually, and hurricanes account for 60 injuries and 17 deaths each year.

Weather forecasting and severe weather prediction provide opportune warnings to the public. An accurate and efficient weather data visualization tool can significantly help forecasters gain fresh insights on atmospheric conditions and formations, enhancing the accuracy of forecasting simulation models.

### 1.1. NEXRAD Level II Doppler Radar Data

The National Weather Surveillance 1988 Doppler Radar Network (WSR-88D), also known as the Next Generation Weather radar system (NEXRAD), provides high spatial and temporal resolution information within 3D volumes on a continuous basis. The network comprises over 150 radar sites across the United States and some overseas locations. The raw datasets consist of three basic meteorological fields: reflectivity, radial velocity, and spectrum width. Reflectivity is related to weather phenomena such as clouds and rain, velocity is germane to wind, and spectrum width is relevant to turbulence. Reflectivity is the primary focus of this study.

### 1.2. Statement of the Problem

Developing a weather visualization tool involves several problems:

1. Measured weather datasets contain measurement errors (e.g., NEXRAD Level II data has a large proportion of missing or invalid data points). Existing packages usually ignore these errors.
2. Radar datasets are captured in 3D, but most displays are limited to 2D images, 3D point clouds, or iso-surfaces, which cannot accurately represent the entire field.
3. Displaying multiple Doppler radar datasets and long-time datasets is challenging due to:
   - Current approaches mosaic 2D images from single sites
   - Asynchronous data files from different stations (temporal aliasing)
   - Heavy computation load for long sequences

### 1.3. Purpose of the Study

The goal is the development of a real-time visualization system to render 3D Doppler radar data from multiple sites. A case study on supercell storms observed on March 12, 2006, in the Midwest of the United States uses data from three Weather Surveillance Doppler radars: KEAX (Kansas City, Missouri), KILX (Lincoln, Illinois), and KSLX (Saint Louis, Missouri).

### 1.4. Delimitations

- Focuses on NEXRAD Level II Doppler radar data only
- Does not handle datasets from other sources (e.g., geostationary satellites)
- Field of interest is reflectivity only
- Limited to visualization from three radar stations
- Emphasizes hardware-accelerated volumetric rendering techniques (not iso-surfacing)

### 1.5. Summary

The system integrates data from multiple sites into an entire volume at every timestamp, stored as a 3D texture. Hardware-accelerated texture-based volume rendering speeds up rendering. Compression and decompression procedures for 3D textures reduce disk space and improve rendering performance while maintaining image quality.

## Chapter 2: Related Work

### 2.1. Weather Visualization Systems

- **IDV (Integrated Data Viewer):** Open source Java-based framework by Unidata (NSF-funded). Reads satellite imagery, gridded data, surface observations, balloon soundings, WSR-88D Level II/III radar data, and NOAA Profiler Network data.
- **NCDC Java NEXRAD Viewer:** Loads Level II, III, and Stage III radar data into an Open GIS-compliant environment.
- **IRAS (Interactive Radar Analysis System):** Part of the CRAFT project, reads and displays Level II radar data via the Internet in real time.
- **Vis5D (Hibbard 1990):** Interactive visualization of large grid datasets from numerical weather models. Five dimensions: 3 spatial, time, and one physical variable. Supports iso-surfaces, contour-line slices, colored slices, and volume renditions.
- **VisAD (Hibbard 1998):** Java class component library for geographically distributed users with interactive and collaborative visualizations.
- **Georgia Tech System (Jiang 2001):** Real-time acquisition, organization, and visualization of atmospheric datasets in a geospatial environment (VGIS).
- **Deep Thunder (IBM, Treinish 2004):** Meso-scale numerical prediction and visualization system, complementary to NWS.
- **Riley et al. (2003, 2004):** Visualization system based on particles' optical properties (extinction and scattering) with multiple fields (cloud water, ice, rain, snow, graupel).
- **Song et al. (2006):** Integrated atmospheric visual analysis and exploration system with physics-based rendering, illustrative rendering, and particle/glyph rendering.
- **Schpok et al. (2003):** "Swell" — multi-level, interactive volumetric cloud modeling and animation system.
- **Ueng et al. (2005):** Three-pass technique for Doppler radar data: re-sampling/filtering, velocity computation/interpolation, and splatting volume rendering.
- **VORTRAC (Harasti 2006):** Analyzes hurricane strength at the National Hurricane Center.

### 2.2. Volume Rendering Techniques in Weather Visualization

Four general approaches for volume rendering:
- **Image-ordered ray casting** (Levoy 1988)
- **Object-ordered splatting** (Westover 1990)
- **Shear-warping** (Lacroute 1994)
- **Texture-based volume rendering** (Wilson 1994)

Key contributions:
- Kajiya & Von Herzen (1984): Ray tracing with single and multiple scattering
- Nishita et al. (1996): Global illumination approximation for multiple anisotropic scattering
- Dobashi et al. (2000): Near real-time cloud animation using splatting
- Harris (2001, 2002, 2003): Realistic real-time cloud rendering with multiple forward scattering
- Ebert et al. (1990): Combined scanline A-buffer with volume rendering
- Jang et al. (2002): View-dependent multiresolution splatting
- Engel et al. (2001): Hardware-accelerated pre-integrated volume rendering

### 2.3. Doppler Data Processing

- Chen et al. (2001A, 2001B): Local least square and regularization frameworks for 3D velocity computation
- Qiu et al. (2001): Extended 2D tracking algorithm to 3D for Doppler reflectivity data
- Zhou et al. (2005): Wind field velocity calculation from single Doppler radar
- Laroche & Zawadzki (1994): Variational method for 3D wind field construction
- Gao et al. (2001): 3D wind field retrieval using adjoint method
- Djurcilov & Pang (1999): Techniques for visualizing datasets with missing values

### 2.4. Data Compression Algorithms

**Lossless compression:**
- Run-length encoding (RLE)
- Prediction by partial matching (PPM)
- Burrows-Wheeler transform (BWT)
- Huffman entropy encoding

**Lossy compression:**
- Fractal transform
- Wavelet compression
- Vector quantization
- S3 Texture compression (S3TC, DXTn) — fixed-rate, single memory access
- Volume texture compression (VTC) — optimized for 3D textures

### 2.5. Conclusion

Current systems enable interactive visualization via various rendering techniques. Future improvements include advanced transfer functions, flexible ground mapping, multi-field properties, and hardware advancements. Visualization of multiple Doppler radar datasets provides larger views with higher resolution, but sequential long-time dataset visualization remains largely unsolved.

## Chapter 3: Procedures

The system consists of two major components: data processing and rendering.

### 3.1. Data Processing

Raw NEXRAD Level II Doppler data are compressed binary files stored in local spherical coordinate systems. Processing steps:

1. Retrieve compressed radar data from files captured at approximately the same time
2. Convert local spherical coordinates to global geographic coordinates
3. Align sample values by linear interpolation from multiple sites
4. Re-sample values into a rectilinear grid structure
5. Compress and store the 3D volumetric data on disk

#### 3.1.1. Radar Data Extraction

Radars perform continuous 360° rotation in azimuth with simultaneous elevation increase of 1° to 3° per sweep. Spatial resolution: 1 km for reflectivity, 0.25 km for velocity and spectrum width in range; 1° for all fields in azimuth. Up to 20 azimuthal scans from 0.5° to 19.5° elevation. Temporal resolution: ~5-6 minutes in severe weather.

Raw datasets are processed using the TRMM Radar Software Library (RSL).

#### 3.1.2. 3D Rectilinear Grid

A bounding box covering all three sites is generated. The grid has a non-uniform structure (e.g., 256×256×128) with 128 layers of X-Y planes, each a 2D uniform grid of 256×256. This is referred to as "semi-regular."

#### 3.1.3. Conversion from Spherical Coordinates to Geographic Coordinates

Given radar site location `(lon, lat, alt)` and sample point local spherical coordinates `(r, θ, φ)`:

```
lon' = lon + O'A'·cos(φ) / lonScale
lat' = lat + O'A'·sin(φ) / latScale
alt' = OA - Re
```

Where `Re ≈ 6,367,450.0 m`, `latScale = Re·π/180`, `lonScale = Re·cos(lat)·π/180`.

Cell indices are computed from the bounding box extents.

#### 3.1.4. Integrating Data from Multiple Sites

Radars at different locations operate at different tempos. Linear interpolation between timestamps synchronizes data:

```
vt = vta·(1 - (t-ta)/(tb-ta)) + vtb·((t-ta)/(tb-ta))
```

Noise attenuation removes extreme values (data limited to 0-80 dBZ).

#### 3.1.5. Constructing and Compressing the Volume

In overlapping regions, values from multiple sites are averaged. Vertical interpolation fills gaps. A modified Run Length Encoding (RLE) algorithm compresses the data:

- 8-bit byte represents reflectivity value
- First bit: 0 = repetition (followed by 32-bit unsigned integer count), 1 = non-repetitive (value OR 128)

### 3.2. Weather Data Rendering

Texture-based volume rendering approach is adopted over polygon mesh representation (which leaves gaps between cone formations and is computationally expensive for multiple sites).

#### 3.2.1. Texture-based Volume-Rendering Technique

The optical model (from Engel 2006):

```
I(sd) = I0·e^(-∫τ(t)dt) + ∫L(s)τ(s)·e^(-∫τ(t)dt) ds
```

Back-to-front compositing:

```
C'dst ← (1-αsrc)Cdst + Csrc·αsrc
α'dst ← (1-αsrc)αdst + αsrc
```

#### 3.2.2. Decompression and 3D Textures

Decompression reverses the RLE encoding. The 1D array of voxel data is transferred to a 3D texture in GPU memory.

#### 3.2.3. Viewport-aligned Parallel Slices

The 3D volume is represented as a unit cube divided into viewport-aligned slices in back-to-front order, parallel to the image plane. Orthogonal projection ensures consistent sampling rate. Default slice count: 256 (user-adjustable).

#### 3.2.4. Transfer Functions

A 1D look-up color table (based on NWS radar reflectivity color table) maps scalar reflectivity values to color and opacity. An interactive transfer function window allows users to create and modify transfer functions using cubic Hermite spline (Cardinal Spline) interpolation.

#### 3.2.5. CG Shading and Animation

NVIDIA CG shading programming language is used for vertex and fragment shaders. The GUI allows user interaction. 3D textures are stored on disk at 5-minute intervals for animation playback.

## Chapter 4: Results

**Test Hardware:** Windows desktop with 3.20GHz Pentium 4, 2.0 GB RAM, NVIDIA Quadro FX 3500 (256 MB video memory). Image resolution: 796×532.

### 4.1. Visualization Results

Four rendering images at different timestamps (00:10:00, 06:10:00, 18:10:00, 23:10:00) show changing weather patterns. Different iso-values produce different observations.

### 4.2. Quality of Visualization

#### 4.2.1. Effect of Volume Resolutions

Three resolutions tested: 128×128×64, 256×256×128, 512×512×256. Lower resolution results in blurriness; higher resolution captures finer details.

#### 4.2.2. Effects of Sampling Rates

Sampling planes varied: 64, 128, 256, 512 (fixed 256×256×128 volume). Fewer slices reduce image quality; more slices improve quality.

### 4.3. Costs and Performance

#### 4.3.1. Effects of Volume Resolution on Data Processing Costs

| Resolution | Avg Processing Time (sec) | Total Processing Time (min) |
|---|---|---|
| 128×128×64 | 6 | 30 |
| 256×256×128 | 8 | 45 |
| 512×512×256 | 12 | 57 |

#### 4.3.2. Effects of Volume Resolution on Data Rendering Costs

| Resolution | Read Texture | Update Texture | Actual Rendering | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 128×128×64 | <1 ms (—) | 3.8 ms (3.02%) | 122 ms (96.98%) | 126 ms |
| 256×256×128 | 8 ms (5.19%) | 24 ms (15.58%) | 122 ms (79.22%) | 154 ms |
| 512×512×256 | 100 ms (28.01%) | 131 ms (36.69%) | 126 ms (35.29%) | 357 ms |

Actual rendering on GPU is largely independent of volume resolution.

#### 4.3.3. Performance of Combining Different Resolutions and Sampling Rates

| Slices | 128×128×64 | 256×256×128 | 512×512×256 |
|---|---|---|---|
| 64 | 20 fps | 18 fps | 4 fps |
| 128 | 15 fps | 12 fps | 4 fps |
| 256 | 8 fps | 8 fps | 3.5 fps |
| 512 | 5 fps | 5 fps | 2.5 fps |
| 1024 | 3 fps | 3 fps | 1.5 fps |

Key findings:
- Higher resolution reduces frame rate (more dramatic at lower sampling rates)
- More slices reduce frame rate (more substantial at lower resolutions)

#### 4.3.4. Testing with Different Hardware

Best performance: Dual-Core AMD Opteron 2.2GHz, 2GB RAM, NVIDIA GeForce 8800GTX 768MB — ~45 fps at 128×128×64 with 256 slices.

#### 4.3.5. Effects of the 3D Texture Compression Algorithm

| Resolution | Before Compression | After Compression | Compression Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| 128×128×64 | 1 MB | 10.2 KB | 99.1% |
| 256×256×128 | 8 MB | 54.0 KB | 99.34% |
| 512×512×256 | 64 MB | 308.4 KB | 99.53% |

### 4.4. Summary

Volume resolution determines texture reading/updating time and data processing time on CPU. Number of slices regulates actual rendering time on GPU. Both must be balanced for optimal visualization.

## Chapter 5: Conclusions and Future Work

### 5.1. Summary

Advantages of the system:
- Data integrated from three radar sites into one volume; integrating more sites does not suffer from limited GPU memory
- 3D display with fast rendering via graphics hardware
- Compressed 3D textures save ~99% of disk space and speed up rendering

### 5.2. Limitations

- Field studied is limited to reflectivity
- Data processing is done beforehand (not real-time)
- 3D texture-based rendering becomes expensive for large-scale volumes
- System runs on standalone Windows machine; not feasible for multiple simultaneous users

### 5.3. Future Work

- Construct and visualize other meteorological fields (velocity, spectrum width)
- Adaptive volumetric rendering with hierarchical volume
- Lighting effects for volume samples
- Integration into TeraGrid with SRB distributed file systems
- Web portal via VNC for remote access
- Distributed computing for processing and rendering jobs
- Pre-processing for other simulation systems (e.g., ParaView)
