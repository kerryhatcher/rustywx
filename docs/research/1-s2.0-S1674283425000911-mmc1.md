Figure S1 compares the ERA5 data on the 500 hPa wind field and geopotential height with the simulation results from the outermost 4 km layer of the model, just before the Foshan tornado occurred on October 4, 2015, at 0700 UTC. From the ERA5 data (Figure S1a) [source PDF prints "Figure 1a" here; the "S" prefix is added for consistency with the supplement's figure numbering, cf. the verbatim "Figure S1b" later in this paragraph], two east-west oriented upper-level jet streams can be observed at 500 hPa, located near 37Â°N and 33Â°N, respectively. In the model simulation results (Figure S1b), the distribution of the two jet streams is very close to that seen in the ERA5 data, which confirms the accuracy of the simulation in reproducing large-scale wind field structures. Despite this, the overall simulated circulation field shows only minor differences when compared to the ERA5 data, proving the reliability of the outermost layer's simulation.

Using 49m resolution data to simulate tornadoes, we propose a conceptual diagram for tornado genesis and development, as shown in Figure S4. During the developmental stage of a tornado, horizontal vortex tubes influence the tilting term in the vertical vorticity equation, causing horizontal vorticity to convert into vertical vorticity under the effect of vertical velocity. This leads to an increase in vertical vorticity, which further enhances rotational flow. Additionally, the vorticity associated with horizontal vortex tubes influences the vertical pressure gradient force, indirectly promoting enhanced vertical motion, thus leading to further tornado development.

## Text S2 â€” Derivation of Equation (4) in the Main Text

Equation (1) is the vertical pressure-gradient force equation:

$$\eta = -\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} \tag{1}$$

By substituting Equation (1) into $\dfrac{d\eta}{dt}$, one obtains:

$$\frac{d\eta}{dt} = \frac{\partial \eta}{\partial t} + u\frac{\partial \eta}{\partial x} + v\frac{\partial \eta}{\partial y} + w\frac{\partial \eta}{\partial t}\,z$$
$$= -\frac{1}{\rho^{2}}\left(\rho u\frac{\partial^{2}p}{\partial x\,\partial z} + \rho v\frac{\partial^{2}p}{\partial y\,\partial z} + \rho w\frac{\partial^{2}p}{\partial z^{2}} - u\frac{\partial p}{\partial z}\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial x} - v\frac{\partial p}{\partial z}\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial y} - w\frac{\partial p}{\partial z}\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial z} + \rho\frac{\partial^{2}p}{\partial t\,\partial z} - \frac{\partial p}{\partial z}\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}\right) \tag{2}$$

The primitive equations in a local Cartesian coordinate system without assuming hydrostatic or geostrophic balance:

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + u\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial x} + v\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial y} + w\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial z} + \rho\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial z}\right) = 0 \tag{3}$$

$$\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial t} + u\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} + v\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} + w\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial z} = ST \tag{4}$$

From Equation (3), one can obtain:

$$\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = -u\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial x} - v\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial y} - w\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial z} - \rho\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} - \rho\frac{\partial v}{\partial y} - \rho\frac{\partial w}{\partial z} \tag{5}$$

The formulas for potential temperature and the equation of state are:

$$\theta = T\left(\frac{p_s}{p}\right)^{\frac{R}{c_p}} \tag{6}$$

$$p = \rho R T \tag{7}$$

From Equations (6) and (7), one obtains:

$$\left(\frac{p_s}{p}\right)^{\frac{R}{c_p}} = \frac{\theta \rho R}{p} \tag{8}$$

The three-dimensional divergence equation is:

$$\lambda = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial z} \tag{9}$$

By using Equations (4) and (8), one obtains:

$$\frac{\theta}{p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} - \frac{\theta}{\rho}\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} - \frac{\theta R}{pc_p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} + \frac{u\theta}{p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} - \frac{u\theta}{\rho}\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial x} - \frac{u\theta R}{pc_p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} + \frac{v\theta}{p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial y}$$
$$- \frac{v\theta}{\rho}\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial y} - \frac{v\theta R}{pc_p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial y} + \frac{w\theta}{p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} - \frac{w\theta}{\rho}\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial z} - \frac{w\theta R}{pc_p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} = ST \tag{10}$$

By using Equations (5) and (10), one obtains:

$$\theta\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \theta\frac{\partial w}{\partial z} - \frac{\theta u c_v}{pc_p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} - \frac{\theta v c_v}{pc_p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial y} - \frac{\theta w c_v}{pc_p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} + \theta\frac{\partial v}{\partial y} - \frac{\theta c_v}{pc_p}\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = ST \tag{11}$$

By moving the term $\dfrac{\partial p}{\partial t}$ from the right-hand side of Equation (11) to the left-hand side, one obtains:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = \frac{pc_p}{c_v}\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{pc_p}{c_v}\frac{\partial v}{\partial y} + \frac{pc_p}{c_v}\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}z - u\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} - v\frac{\partial p}{\partial y} - w\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} - \frac{STpc_p}{\theta c_v} \tag{12}$$

Using Equation (12), one obtains:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} + u\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} + v\frac{\partial p}{\partial y} + w\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} = \frac{pc_p}{c_v}\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{pc_p}{c_v}\frac{\partial v}{\partial y} + \frac{pc_p}{c_v}\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}z - \frac{STpc_p}{\theta c_v} \tag{13}$$

By moving the term $\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}$ from the right-hand side of Equation (9) to its left-hand side, one obtains:

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial w}{\partial z} + \lambda \tag{14}$$

Using Equations (13) and (14), one obtains:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} + u\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} + v\frac{\partial p}{\partial y} + w\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} = \frac{pc_p\lambda}{c_v} - \frac{STpc_p}{\theta c_v} \tag{15}$$

Equation (15) can be transformed into:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{c_v\rho\theta}\left(pc_p\lambda\rho\theta - c_v\rho\theta u\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} - c_v\rho\theta v\frac{\partial p}{\partial y} - c_v\rho\theta w\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} - STp\rho c_p\right) \tag{16}$$

The pressure sourceâ€“sink formula is:

$$Sp = -\frac{STpc_p}{\theta c_v} \tag{17}$$

Using Equations (16) and (17), one obtains:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = \frac{pc_p\lambda}{c_v} - u\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} - v\frac{\partial p}{\partial y} - w\frac{\partial p}{\partial z} + Sp \tag{18}$$

By substituting Equations (5) and (18) into Equation (2) and combining like terms, one obtains Equation (19):

$$\frac{d\eta}{dt} = \frac{\partial p}{\partial z}\left(-\frac{c_p\lambda}{\rho c_v} - \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial v}{\partial y} - \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right) - \frac{pc_p}{\rho c_v}\frac{\partial \lambda}{\partial z} + \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial v}{\partial z}\frac{\partial p}{\partial y} - \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial Sp}{\partial z} \tag{19}$$

Since the horizontal pressure-gradient force in the x-direction is $\alpha = -\dfrac{1}{\rho}\dfrac{\partial p}{\partial x}$ and in the y-direction is $\beta = -\dfrac{1}{\rho}\dfrac{\partial p}{\partial y}$, substituting these into Equation (19) yields:

$$\frac{d\eta}{dt} = \eta\left(\frac{c_p\lambda}{c_v} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right) - \frac{pc_p}{\rho c_v}\frac{\partial \lambda}{\partial z} - \alpha\frac{\partial u}{\partial z} - \beta\frac{\partial v}{\partial z} - \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial Sp}{\partial z} \tag{20}$$

Substituting Equation (9) into Equation (20) and then splitting and recombining terms yields Equation (21), which corresponds to Equation (4) in the main text:

$$\frac{d\eta}{dt} = \frac{\eta c_p\lambda}{c_v} + \eta\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\right) + \eta\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right) - \left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial z}\right)\alpha - \left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial z}\right)\beta - \frac{pc_p\left(\dfrac{\partial}{\partial z}\left(\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \dfrac{\partial v}{\partial y}\right)\right)}{\rho c_v} - \frac{pc_p\left(\dfrac{\partial}{\partial z}\left(\dfrac{\partial w}{\partial z}\right)\right)}{\rho c_v} - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\frac{Sp}{\rho} \tag{21}$$

**Symbol notes (as used in Text S2):** $\eta$ = vertical component of the pressure-gradient/vorticity-related term defined by Eq. (1); $\rho$ = air density; $p$ = pressure; $u, v, w$ = wind components in $x$ (east), $y$ (north), $z$ (vertical); $\theta$ = potential temperature; $T$ = temperature; $R$ = gas constant for dry air; $c_p, c_v$ = specific heats at constant pressure/volume; $p_s$ = reference (surface) pressure; $ST$ = diabatic heating source/sink term for potential temperature; $Sp$ = derived pressure sourceâ€“sink term (Eq. 17); $\lambda$ = three-dimensional divergence (Eq. 9); $\alpha, \beta$ = horizontal pressure-gradient force components in $x$ and $y$.
