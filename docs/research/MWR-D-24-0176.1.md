## ABSTRACT

An EF1 tornado was documented using photographs, a high-resolution video, and a mobile radar as it entered Selden, KS on 24 May 2021. The kinematic structure of the tornadic wind field was presented by tracking lofted debris and analyzing single-Doppler velocities. Tracking of debris on the side of the tornado farthest from the observer was possible due to the transparent nature of the debris cloud. The analysis suggests that the circulation was axisymmetric with the maximum horizontal velocities located at low levels. The positive vertical velocities were strongest on the forward side of the tornado. The maximum vertical velocities were associated with a secondary vortex. For the first time, the data set provided an opportunity to assess the orientation of a large, lofted debris based on the images recorded by a movie and compare these observations with the differential radar reflectivity (Z<sub>DR</sub>) recorded by a mobile polarimetric radar. T-matrix calculations of wood boards yielded a mean Z<sub>DR</sub> that was negative and was also observed in the Z<sub>DR</sub> analysis suggesting a preference for lofted debris to be vertically oriented.

## 1. Introduction

There have been numerous attempts to document the wind/debris speeds accompanying tornadoes over the past six decades. Early studies relied on speed estimates based on photogrammetric analyses of movies of the lofted debris cloud. The visual characteristic of the cloud is dependent on the type of lofted debris and the wind speeds associated with the tornado (e.g., Lewellen et al. 2008). The analysis requires tracking debris, elements of clouds, or dust over a known period of time using a grid of photogrammetrically-derived elevation and azimuth angles that is superimposed on top of the images. Hoecker (1960) was among the first to present analyses of the tangential and vertical velocity components of cloud fragments and debris associated with a tornado. He assumed that the debris was moving at the same velocity as the tornado wind speeds which is known to be unlikely (e.g., Dowell et al. 2005; Bodine et al. 2016; Cheong et al. 2017; Umeyama et al. 2018). Although the profiles of speeds shown by Hoecker (1960) were detailed, the methodology used to determine the wind/debris speeds was not documented, so the assumptions and possible errors in his analysis could not be ascertained. Fujita (1975), Golden and Purcell (1978), and Campbell et al. (1983) have also attempted to analyze the tornadic wind field using movies of the debris field. The debris speeds were typically measured in the outer portion of an opaque dust cloud closest to the camera in intense tornadoes. An overview of photogrammetric studies of tornadoes is presented by Golden (1976), Bluestein and Golden (1979), and Forbes and Bluestein (2001).

The past few decades have seen a decrease in the number of photogrammetric estimates of speeds and a subsequent rise in the number of Doppler velocity estimates of tornado debris motion using mobile radars (e.g., Wurman and Gill 2000; Wurman 2002; Bluestein et al. 2004, 2007a; Lee and Wurman 2005; Kosiba and Wurman 2010; Wakimoto et al. 2011; Wurman and Kosiba 2013; Wurman et al. 2013, 2014, Griffin et al. 2017). The introduction of polarimetric radars has increased our understanding of the scatterers by discriminating between types of hydrometeors and identifying regions of lofted debris (e.g., Ryzhkov, et al. 2005; Bluestein et al. 2007b, 2015; Kumjian and Ryzhkov 2008; Bodine et al. 2013, 2014; Snyder and Bluestein 2014; Kurdzo et al. 2015; Houser et al. 2016; Tanamachi et al. 2012; VanDen Broeke 2015; Wakimoto et al. 2015, 2016, 2018; Griffin et al. 2020).

In light of the numerous studies that have tracked tornadic debris using movies/videos and the estimates of debris velocity based on high-resolution Doppler velocity measurements of tornadoes using mobile radars, it is surprising that there has been no study to date that has applied both techniques on a single tornado. The two estimates of debris motion are complementary since the cross-beam and radial components of motion are determined by these methods, respectively. Of course, the former is based on the motion of lofted debris that is large enough to be tracked in a sequence of images while the latter is based on the average motion of a spectrum of debris sizes and associated electromagnetic scattering characteristics within the radar pulse volume (Bodine et al. 2016).

A supercell developed on 24 May 2021 near Selden, Kansas, and subsequently spawned a tornado. The tornado was located ~4 km from the deployment site of a mobile rapidly-scanning X-band polarimetric radar (RaXPol; Pazmany et al. 2013). A high-resolution video of the tornado was also recorded at the radar site when numerous lofted debris particles were noted. The goal of the present study includes reconstructing the kinematic windfield of the tornado based on a combination of lofted debris tracking and single-Doppler velocity estimates of a tornado circulation. The vertical velocities based on the vertical debris displacements in a video can be approximated if the terminal fall speed of the debris can be estimated. In addition, the visible orientation of large debris can be compared with the differential radar reflectivity, Z<sub>DR</sub>, to examine whether the polarimetric measurements are consistent with the observations. A detailed aerial and ground survey was undertaken on 25 May in order to rate the tornado damage intensity and document the types of debris that were lofted by the strong winds. Section 2 describes RaXPol, photogrammetric techniques used to analyze a high-resolution video, and the superposition of the damage survey with the data collected during the quasi-horizontal scans from the radar. Vertical cross sections of the polarimetric data through the tornado, tracking of lofted debris based on a photogrammetric analysis of the video, and an analysis of differential radar reflectivity, Z<sub>DR</sub>, with observed debris orientation is presented in section 3. Section 4 presents a summary and discussion.

## 2. RaXPol, photogrammetry, and the damage survey

RaXPol is a dual-polarization X-band radar that transmits at a wavelength of 3.1 cm. The diameter of the antenna is 2.4 m, and the half power (3 dB) beamwidth is 1°. The antenna can rotate as rapidly as 180° s<sup>-1</sup>. The range resolution used during the case examined herein is 150 m; however, it is oversampled such that the range gates were 60 m. The frequency-diversity technique was used in order to increase the number of independent samples (Hildebrand and Moore 1990). The elevation angles within a volume scan were 0° to 18° in 2° steps, and each volume was completed in ~20 s. RaXPol was deployed at the site shown in Fig. 1 from 2304:43–2320:42 UTC (UTC=CDT+5h). The radar location was within a few kilometers of the Selden tornado as it formed and traversed through the city. The interested reader is referred to Pazmany et al. (2013) for additional information on RaXPol.

A high-resolution video recorded the Selden tornado lofting debris into the air at the same time RaXPol collected data on the circulation. Photogrammetry has been used by a number of researchers in order to obtain quantitative information from a photograph or a movie (e.g., Malkus 1952; Bluestein 1986; Wakimoto and Martner 1992; and Zehnder et al. 2007); an overview of photogrammetry can be found in Abrams (1952) and Holle (1986). Accordingly, a series of images of the Selden tornado were used to track the debris trajectories. The analysis of the images requires knowledge of the precise location of the camera and the azimuth angles to targets identified in the image horizon. Subsequently, calculations using photogrammetry can be used to determine the tilt angle (τ) and the effective focal length (EFL, Fig. 2). The tilt angle is the angular distance from the horizontal plane, and the EFL is the distance from the camera to the principal point (the center of the photograph). The tilt angle and the EFL must be known in order to construct an azimuth- and elevation-angle grid using spherical trigonometry. The grid is superimposed onto each image and is analogous to the radar scanning angles since the movie was recorded at the same location as the deployment site. The calculated azimuth-angle grid was compared to the azimuth angles of the known targets on the horizon to determine the angle errors that ranged between 0.1° and 0.2° or 7-14 m during the analysis time shown for this case study. The raw radar data were plotted and analyzed on vertical cross sections through the center of the tornado.

A time sequence of the hook echo approaching Selden based on the low-level scans from the WSR-88D located at Goodland, Kansas, is presented in Fig. 1. ## 3. Vertical cross sections through the tornado

### a. Multiparameter analysis

High resolution photographs and videos were taken of the Selden tornado. A funnel cloud was noted at select times (not shown), but there were also instances when it was absent (Fig. 9a). Secondary vortices were also apparent as noted in Fig. 9a. The photos and videos captured individual lofted debris that was circulating within the tornado. The Selden tornado was relatively weak, so the amount of lofted debris was not significant. Accordingly, the debris cloud was transparent which allowed for a rare opportunity to track debris along the periphery of the tornado that was both closest to and farthest from the radar site. The radar scanning volume from 2318:31-2318:35 UTC was selected since it matched a period when large pieces of debris could be identified in the video.

The range to the rotational couplet was determined using the Doppler velocity data. A pseudo-vertical cross section was constructed at this range through the center of the tornado and the weak-echo column (WEC; Tanamachi et al. 2012) using the raw data collected by RaXPol. The use of raw data rather than interpolated data helped preserve the finescale detail of the radar analysis. A constant range was used so the cross sections are along curved surfaces as illustrated in Fig. 7a. The polarimetric variables are subject to statistical errors in regions characterized by low signal-to-noise ratio. As a result, it is possible that small shifts in the location of the vertical cross section could lead to significant changes in the analyses. To overcome these errors, a five-range gate average (i.e., averaging over 300 m in the radial direction) of the polarimetric variables was performed and plotted on the cross section.

The WEC is apparent in Fig. 9b and is located near the center of the rotational couplet (Figs. 3b, 3c, 9c). The WEC was subjectively defined as radar reflectivities <20 dBZ (light blue shaded region in Fig. 9b). The asymmetry in the rotational couplet noted earlier can be seen in the figure. The maximum azimuthal shear is ~0.15 s⁻¹. A weakening secondary vortex can be seen in the photograph taken at 2318:31 (Fig. 9a). The weak circulation may explain why there is no apparent smaller rotational couplet in the single-Doppler velocity image even at close range to the radar (Fig. 7b). However, the secondary vortex was positioned in a region of strong cyclonic shear (Fig. 9c). The primary area of lofted debris based on the low ρ<sub>hv</sub> is located between the 243° and 249° azimuths (Figs. 9d and f). The minimum in ρ<sub>hv</sub> is near the ground (245.0°–245.5°) within a region of high radar reflectivity (Fig. 9b) suggesting that large amounts of debris are being lofted near the surface. The radar reflectivities decrease at higher levels within the region of low ρ<sub>hv</sub> which suggests that the lofted debris has either fallen out or been centrifuged from the circulation. Another area of relatively low ρ<sub>hv</sub> is near the secondary vortex. The vertical profile of Z<sub>DR</sub> (Fig. 9e) is characterized by two columns of negative Z<sub>DR</sub> near the regions of low ρ<sub>hv</sub>. Columns of negative Z<sub>DR</sub> within lofted debris have been noted before (e.g., Wakimoto et al. 2018, 2022) and were proposed to result from some degree of common debris alignment (Cheong et al. 2017; Umeyama et al. 2018).

There have been several studies in the refereed literature that have presented the vertical structure of the multiparameter analysis superimposed onto a photograph of the tornado. These analyses have shown that the WEC appears to be ubiquitous (e.g., Wakimoto and Martner 1992; Wurman and Gill 2000; Wakimoto et al. 2011, 2015, 2016, 2018). Columns of negative Z<sub>DR</sub> within lofted debris have been observed (e.g., Wakimoto et al. 2018, 2022) and suggest that regions of common alignment are more common than previously believed. The vertical regions of low ρ<sub>hv</sub> have been consistent with the lofted debris that is visually apparent, but also identified areas of relatively small debris that could not be identified in the high-resolution videos/pictures (e.g., Wakimoto et al. 2018). The evolution of the vertical profile of ρ<sub>hv</sub> through a debris cloud is dynamic as a result of the constant infusion of debris into the tornadic circulation at the surface and the centrifuging of debris out of the vortex. A vertical column of large ρ<sub>hv</sub> centered along the tornado vertical axis has been noted due to the latter effect. The lofted debris accompanying the weak tornado in the present case resulted in an asymmetric pattern of ρ<sub>hv</sub> (Fig. 9d and f). This asymmetry was likely caused by the weaker ground-relative tangential winds on the north side of the tornado that did not loft large quantities of debris except at the location of the secondary vortex.

### b. Tracking debris

The video recorded at the same location as the RaXPol deployment site provided continuous visual coverage of the Selden tornado. Numerous examples of lofted debris that could be tracked were identified in the images. This is believed to be the first time that debris trajectories based on photogrammetry and single-Doppler velocities recorded by a radar have been simultaneously collected on the same tornado. As already noted, the region of low ρ<sub>hv</sub> indicates the presence of debris (Fig. 10a), but the amount lofted was not significant since the tornado was relatively weak. Accordingly, the lofted debris cloud was transparent which resulted in debris being tracked from south to north (left to right) on the side of the circulation closest to the radar but also north to south (right to left) on the far side of the circulation (Fig. 10a). The tracks plotted on Fig. 10a include lofted debris identified in the video from 2318:19 – 2318:43 which is ±22 secs from the photo time (2318:31).

Every lofted debris that could be resolved in the video during this time interval was tracked. Accordingly, approximately one hundred debris trajectories were analyzed. The majority of these tracks were short-lived (many of the objects were only tracked for 1-2 s). However, these trajectories were consistent with the tracks shown in Fig. 10. These additional tracks were not included since they did not reveal new information about debris paths and would have impeded the clarity of the presentation in Fig. 10. Other time periods in the video were also examined, but the one shown in the present study was chosen due to the well-defined debris trajectories and simultaneous collection of multiparameter radar data during the volume scan. This time period provided a unique opportunity to compare the single-Doppler velocities with the photogrammetrically derived velocities from tracking the lofted debris.

The debris tracks moving to the north (yellow isopleths) are primarily ascending in the tornadic updrafts. A number of the tracks moving to the south (blue isopleths) suggest descending motion. These observations provide evidence that the positive vertical velocities were stronger along the leading edge of the tornado. A schematic model was drawn to aid in the interpretation of the debris trajectories that are being tracked in the video (Fig. 10b). The single-Doppler velocities represent the debris and hydrometeor velocities along the radials from the radar as illustrated in the figure. The debris trajectories that are tracked using photogrammetric techniques are following curved paths within the tornadic circulation. In addition, a positive component of radial motion from debris centrifuging is also illustrated in the figure. Accordingly, the most accurate estimates of the debris velocity in the cross-radial direction will occur near the perpendicular from the image horizon (see Fig. 2) also referred to as the principal point (Holle 1982). The perpendicular is often referred to as the principal line (white line in Fig. 10a). The least accurate estimates of velocities based on tracking debris will be near the right and left edges of the photograph. The curved trajectories from 253°-255° appear to suggest debris recycling when debris is ejected at higher levels and, subsequently, curls downward as it enters the inflow region at lower levels (e.g., Dowell et al. 2005, see their Fig. 12). However, these trajectories represent debris motion that is embedded in the tornadic circulation and is simply changing direction as it enters the northern extent of the debris cloud/circulation as shown in Fig. 10b (note that the change in direction occurs near the 0.80 to 0.90 ρ<sub>hv</sub> isopleths). Additional evidence for this interpretation is that debris recycling is not suggested at other locations based on the trajectories shown in Fig. 10. It would not be expected that recycling would be confined to the right side (north) of the tornado circulation shown in the video.

The maximum Doppler velocities away and towards the radar at low levels were 57.7 m s<sup>-1</sup> (~120 m AGL) and 37.8 m s<sup>-1</sup> (~20 m AGL), respectively (Fig. 12b). The average Doppler velocity is 47.8 m s<sup>-1</sup>. Two estimates of the horizontal velocities of the lofted debris are 34.4 m s<sup>-1</sup> near the surface and approximately 26.6 m s<sup>-1</sup> 75 m above ground level, respectively (tracks #1 and 2 in Fig. 12a). The stronger velocity near the surface is consistent with previous studies (e.g., Kosiba and Wurman 2023). The debris velocities based on photogrammetry are less than the Doppler velocities if it is assumed that the tornadic circulation is axisymmetric. There are two possible explanations for the slower velocities. The circulation may be asymmetric. In addition, the lofted debris particles that are being tracked are larger than other (more numerous) debris swirling in the circulation since they are visibly identifiable in the movie. The measured Doppler velocity is based on the radial movement of a spectrum of debris and hydrometeor types and sizes within the pulse volume. The velocity difference between the air and debris velocities increases with debris size with larger debris typically moving much more slowly than smaller lofted debris (Lewellen et al. 2008). These observations suggest that the Doppler velocities in this case are a combination of the velocities of various scatterers (hydrometeors, small debris, and large wood boards) with the large debris not being the dominant scatterer.

### c. Debris orientation

A segment along one of the debris tracks (highlighted by the red dots along the track in Fig. 12) was selected since it was able to clearly resolve a large piece of debris during the time that the radar was collecting data in the same region. Every frame of the video (29.97 frames per second) was examined during the interval and every fifth frame was plotted in Fig. 13. There were two frames in the sequence when the lofted debris could not be identified in the video. The size of the debris was photogrammetrically estimated to be ~2 m x 2 m (Fig. 13). A representative example of the type of structures that were damaged by the tornado during the analysis time is shown in Fig. 8c. A number of wood panels (~2 m) have been removed from the roof of a building. The size of the panels closely matches the video images (Fig. 13). This close match and the smaller size of other lofted debris in the region identified during the ground and aerial survey suggests that the object shown in Fig. 13 is likely a wood panel.

Vertical velocities of the lofted debris were also estimated for track #1 and 2. The former was +2.4 m s<sup>-1</sup> and the latter was +4.3 and -4.5 m s<sup>-1</sup> (the lofted debris ascended then descended during the period it was tracked). The stronger vertical velocity near the surface is consistent with numerical simulations (e.g., Lewellen et al. 2008). The strongest vertical motion was associated with the debris trajectory (tracks #3 and 4) near a secondary vortex (Fig. 11a). The debris velocities were +28.1 and -25.0 m s<sup>-1</sup>, respectively in Fig. 12a. These vertical velocities of the air are approximately an order of magnitude larger that the velocities associated with tracks #1 and 2. The vertical velocities can be approximated if the debris terminal velocity is known. Dowell et al. (2005) assumed a terminal fall speed of 20 m s<sup>-1</sup> for a plywood sheet. Accordingly, the vertical wind speeds estimated for tracks #1 and 2 were all positive with a maximum of +24.4 m s<sup>-1</sup> and a minimum of +15.5 m s<sup>-1</sup>. The vertical wind speeds accompanying the secondary vortex are +48.1 m s<sup>-1</sup> and -5.0 m s<sup>-1</sup>. The negative debris velocity near the secondary vortex is close to its fall speed assuming it was ejected from the vortex and falling out in an area where vertical velocities were weak.

The blue line in Fig. 13 denotes the approximate debris orientation based on the image in the figure and the examination of adjacent frames of the video (not shown). The wood panel is tumbling based on an examination of the images. There is a tendency for the debris to be more horizontally-oriented during the first 2 s (top row) and more vertically-oriented during the last 2 s (bottom row). The examination of the lofted debris shown in the figure provides an opportunity to compare these observations with the Z<sub>DR</sub> field in this region. The analysis shown in Fig. 12c suggests that the lofted debris was in an area characterized by negative Z<sub>DR</sub>. Comparisons between the video images and the radar Z<sub>DR</sub> analyses should be viewed with some caution since the latter is an average of a pulse volume which contains a spectrum of debris sizes of different orientation.

Since the debris orientation and approximate size can be estimated in Fig. 13, electromagnetic scattering calculations at X band were conducted for a 2-m plywood sheet to study the scattering behavior for different debris orientations.[^1] The thickness of the plywood sheet was assumed to be 2 cm. T-matrix calculations were done assuming an aspect ratio of 0.5 for an oblate spheroid composed of dry wood (2–0.2j), following the methods noted in Bodine et al. (2014) and Cross et al. (2023). While higher fidelity electromagnetic scattering calculations are possible (e.g., Lujan 2016), T-matrix calculations provide reasonable agreement with these other computational methods (Bodine et al. 2023; Cross et al. 2023). The orientation of the wood boards was estimated from Fig. 13, and a mean differential reflectivity (Z<sub>DR</sub>) value was calculated for all observed wood board orientations. To test the sensitivity of debris size, experiments were done for 21 different debris sizes within 10% of the board's volume. The resulting calculation yields a mean Z<sub>DR</sub> of -0.6 dB, in rough agreement with the observed radar signature (Figs. 12c and 14). In addition, 71% of the debris sizes examined yields negative Z<sub>DR</sub>. We note that the instantaneous debris orientation at the radar scan time is unknown, and thus a more direct comparison is not feasible. In addition, the two seconds of data shown in Fig. 13 should not be interpreted as representative of a volume of tumbling scatterers.

[^1]: The orientation of plywood in three dimensions can be determined by the longitudinal, radial, and tangential axes. Orientation in the present study is estimated by the debris major axis in the photographs. The major axis as depicted in the image is the same perspective as the mobile radar.

Mie scattering can make interpretation of positive or negative Z<sub>DR</sub> and its relationship to orientation angle difficult for large debris. When examining the distribution of X-band Z<sub>DR</sub> as a function of debris orientation, a wide range of Z<sub>DR</sub> is possible due to the high aspect ratio of debris, as well as resonance effects for large particles (D > λ/16) where small variations in debris size create complex oscillations in radar reflectivity (Z) and Z<sub>DR</sub>. In fact, for this debris size, Z<sub>DR</sub> tends to have the opposite sign compared to what is expected from its orientation (e.g., horizontally aligned debris has negative Z<sub>DR</sub>), which is illustrated in Fig. 14. Applying the orientations shown in Fig. 13, the first and second 2-s periods (top and bottom rows) have a mean Z<sub>DR</sub> of -4.2 and 3.6 dB, respectively. This complexity is further illustrated in Fig. 15, which shows Z<sub>DR</sub> for horizontally and vertically oriented debris with a spectrum of wood board sizes ranging from a 0.2-m to 2-m lengths at 0.01-m interval. Resonance effects lead to a large variation in Z<sub>DR</sub> and a tendency for horizontally oriented, large debris to have negative Z<sub>DR</sub>. It is noted that the calculations yield positive Z<sub>DR</sub> as expected for small (Rayleigh) scatterers. One discrepancy between the T-matrix Z<sub>DR</sub> and observations is the large magnitudes of Z<sub>DR</sub> seen. Since the resolution volume contains other scatterers with near-zero Z<sub>DR</sub> (e.g., dirt, other debris that may be relatively randomly oriented) or positive Z<sub>DR</sub> (rain drops), the combination of an individually oriented debris particle with these scatterers will tend to have Z<sub>DR</sub> closer to 0 dB.

One remaining question is why debris have some degree of common alignment or consistent orientation with time. Past studies have broadly discussed that debris may have some common orientation, and this was illustrated through debris trajectories in Umeyama et al. (2018). This study illustrated from tracked debris orientations that debris can remain in a near-constant orientation for short periods of time (~1 s). Such patterns have been noted in the wind engineering literature with translating modes of rotation (i.e., lack of rotation) evident in wind tunnel experiments and numerical simulations (e.g., Tachikawa 1983, Fig 16). Tachikawa (1983) has shown that plate-like debris trajectories can have translatory modes where they remain vertically oriented for short time periods, as well as changes in the direction of rotation. Comparison with the present study assumes that the debris are not rotating faster than would cause visual aliasing. A tarp-like debris object exhibited horizontal alignment as it ascended within a subvortex, acting similar to wind directed at a sail (Fig. 17d-g). Non-rigid pieces of debris might have a greater tendency to become aligned. Of course, there are many pieces of debris that are also tumbling rapidly and thus have random or near random orientations.

In addition to the detailed photogrammetric analysis at 23:18:33 UTC, the evolution of the polarimetric TDS (Tornadic Debris Signature, Ryzhkov et al. 2002) is examined as the tornado progresses from an open area into the town of Selden, Kansas. For each time and elevation angle, data are saved within a 1-km radius of the center of the TVS, and the TDS is identified by correlation coefficient less than 0.8. No reflectivity threshold is imposed to include areas within the weak-echo hole or in areas of adjacent fallout (e.g., Griffin et al. 2020).

The visual evolution of the tornado and associated debris cloud are shown in Fig. 17. Examining the period from 2317:51 to 2319:11 UTC (Fig. 17a-c), there is an expansion of the visible debris cloud, and large pieces of debris are lofted. We note that while an increase in large debris concentration is evident, the total number of large debris remains relatively small. In the polarimetric variables (Fig. 18), the mean and 90<sup>th</sup> percentile radar reflectivity factors increase, and the mean and 10<sup>th</sup> percentile correlation coefficient decrease, consistent with expected scattering changes resulting from both increased debris concentrations and sizes (Cross et al. 2023). During the intermediate volume scan (23:18:31 UTC), Z and ρ<sub>hv</sub> exhibit larger vertical gradients with Z decreasing and ρ<sub>hv</sub> increasing with height. In contrast, the mean Z and ρ<sub>hv</sub> are approximately constant with height at the beginning and end of this period (Fig. 18a-b). It is possible that increased low-level debris loading associated with the subvortex contributed to this enhancement in low-level debris concentrations. This is similar to Griffin et al. (2020) who found that low-level debris loading may temporarily result in a vertical gradient in polarimetric variables, and the vertical gradient decreases with time if debris lofting is sustained to create a more homogeneous vertical distribution of debris. However, subvortices were not evident in their case.

The vertical structure of the Z<sub>DR</sub> field exhibits behavior consistent with the observed debris orientations. Mean Z<sub>DR</sub> (Fig. 18c) is near zero during the later times where large debris are evident, and mean Z<sub>DR</sub> tends to increase with height, possibly due to debris fallout and increased influence from hydrometeors. Since mean Z<sub>DR</sub> is near zero or slightly positive, this suggests that the TDS is mostly comprised by randomly oriented debris and/or hydrometeors that have an offsetting influence to the negative Z<sub>DR</sub>. However, since there is a relatively small amount of large, airborne debris, negative or positive Z<sub>DR</sub> associated with visible debris may not heavily influence the mean Z<sub>DR</sub> in the TDS. However, local regions of positive or negative Z<sub>DR</sub> could skew the Z<sub>DR</sub> distribution.

To examine skewness of the Z<sub>DR</sub> distribution, the Pearson's moment coefficient of skewness is calculated to examine a possible positive or negative skewness of the Z<sub>DR</sub> distribution during periods of debris alignment (Fig. 18f). Z<sub>DR</sub> exhibits some negative skewness in the lowest 300 m AGL for most of the period, with the most negative skewness occurring at the lowest scan height. This is consistent with the low altitudes of the debris that are associated with negative Z<sub>DR</sub> shown in Fig. 12c. During the last volume scan (23:19:31 UTC), only the lowest two elevation angles were collected due to a scan mode change, and Z<sub>DR</sub> exhibits a positive skewness. Positive skewness would be expected if the debris field contains some large, horizontally oriented scatterers, and these are evident from the lofted tarp at this time (Fig. 17d-g). Strong vertical motions in the subvortex maintain an orientation of the tarps that tend to be perpendicular to the wind, consistent with simulations by Umeyama et al. (2018) that found some degree of debris alignment perpendicular to the wind direction (e.g., more horizontal alignment in areas of strong vertical motions). In summary, combining the visible evidence and statistical analysis of the TDS, some degree of common debris alignment appears to occur locally within subregions of the TDS where large debris are present. However, the lack of mean negative Z<sub>DR</sub> suggests that these scatterers are not present everywhere, at least in this case.

## 4. Summary and discussion

The current study provided an opportunity to improve our understanding of the kinematic structure of the winds accompanying a tornado by tracking lofted debris and analyzing single-Doppler velocities. The tornado occurred near Selden, Kansas, and was a relatively weak circulation that resulted in EF1 damage in the town. The lofted debris cloud was visible but generally transparent which allowed for tracking debris on both the side of the tornadic circulation closest and farthest from the observer. The debris and Doppler velocities were comparable suggesting that the tangential/rotational velocities were axisymmetric. Tornadoes associated with secondary vortices are inherently asymmetric, but the radial velocities did not resolve the small-scale vortex in this case. The strongest horizontal velocities were located below 50 m. The vertical displacement of the lofted debris consistently suggested that the positive vertical velocities were strongest on the forward side of the tornado. The maximum vertical velocities were associated with a secondary vortex. The key findings of the kinematic structure of the tornado are summarized in Fig. 19.

The orientation of lofted debris within tornadoes has been the subject of recent investigations. Debris orientation was noted for roofing material that was lofted by the Selden tornado at the same time polarimetric data was collected. One large debris remained in a near-constant orientation for a couple of seconds, consistent with wind tunnel experiments. T-matrix calculations of wood boards yielded a mean Z<sub>DR</sub> that was negative for horizontal-oriented debris due to resonance effects. Accordingly, Mie scattering complicates the relationship between positive and negative Z<sub>DR</sub> and the orientation of large, lofted debris even with there is some degree of common alignment. Vertical profiles of the skewness of the Z<sub>DR</sub> distribution within the TDS were negative at the lowest levels suggesting that some degree of common debris alignment is occurring locally. However, the mean Z<sub>DR</sub> is near-zero indicating that the TDS is mostly comprised of randomly oriented debris and/or hydrometeors that offset the influence of the negative Z<sub>DR</sub>.

The current study has improved our understanding of the horizontal velocities within tornadoes by examining four components of the circulation rather than two as documented in past studies. There have been several studies that synthesized the three-dimensional windfield based on dual-Doppler analyses. However, there is a spatiotemporal degradation inherent to the latter technique unlike the present study. Observations of the vertical velocity in tornadoes are rare so the estimates obtained in the Selden tornado help to improve our understanding of this component of the windfield. In the future, stereo photogrammetry of lofted debris cloud would improve the accuracy of the lofted debris trajectories. Analysis of a strong tornado could increase the number of large, lofted debris trajectories and provide additional details of the tornado's kinematic structure, especially at low levels, although the debris cloud may be opaque so that tracking may not be possible on the lee side of the circulation.
