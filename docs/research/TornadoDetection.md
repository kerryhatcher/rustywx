## Slide 4: Weather Radar Basics (1)

![Diagram of WSR-88D Volume Coverage Pattern 11 elevation angles vs. height and range](media/TornadoDetection/page-04.png)

- Doppler radars obtain data by:
  - Transmitting electromagnetic energy in brief pulses at specific angles
  - Energy returns from precipitation, cloud droplets, mountains, etc.
  - Doppler measures strength of return and the component of object motion toward and away from the radar

**Figure — Angles used by the WSR-88D (NWS, 2003):** Volume Coverage Pattern 11 diagram — height (thousands of feet, y-axis, 0–70) vs. range (miles, x-axis, 0–120), showing a fan of colored elevation-angle beams (labeled 19.5, 16.7, 14, 12, 10, 8.7, 7.5, 6.2, 5.3, and lower angles down to 0.5 degrees) spreading upward and outward from the radar location.

## Slide 5: Weather Radar Basics (2)

![Two radar display panels: base reflectivity showing a hook echo, and storm-relative velocity showing rotation, for a supercell thunderstorm](media/TornadoDetection/page-05.png)

- Technically, radar shows larger shear zones, not the actual tornado
- Base reflectivity shows hook echo due to precipitation thrown out of/around rotating updraft core
- Storm relative velocity
  - Speed of wind toward (cool colors) and away (warm colors) from radar
  - The stronger, tighter, & deeper (more than one elevation angle) the rotation, the greater the likelihood a tornado is present
  - Time continuity is also important (more than one volume scan)

**Figures — Supercell Thunderstorm:**
- Top panel: "Base Reflectivity" — color-filled radar reflectivity image showing a classic hook echo signature (yellow/red/high-reflectivity core with a hook-shaped appendage).
- Bottom panel: "Storm Relative Velocity" — color-filled velocity image (green = away, red/orange = toward) with an arrow pointing to a small tight red/green couplet labeled "Tornado embedded here."

## Slide 6: Supercell Tornadoes

![Bullet list slide, no image, describing supercell tornado characteristics](media/TornadoDetection/page-06.png)

- **Occur during a supercell thunderstorm's mature stage**
- Preceded by strong mid-level [15–25,000 feet (ft)] rotation (where radars have a better view)
- In NM, occur mostly along eastern border with TX during May, June, and July
- WSR-88Ds earned excellent reputation with these
- Supercell thunderstorms produce the majority of **NM's confirmed tornadoes each year, but not all of them**

---

## Slide 7: Non-Supercell Tornadoes (a.k.a. landspout, gustnado, or spin-up)

![Bullet list slide, no image, describing non-supercell tornado characteristics](media/TornadoDetection/page-07.png)

- Probably more frequent in Western U.S.
- Often unseen or unreported
- Form early in thunderstorm lifecycle, sometimes before lightning strikes
- May form rapidly near surface then extend upward
- May form simultaneously at low and mid levels
- Shallow and/or narrow (rarely exceed F-2 intensity)
- Larger atmospheric circulations usually cause these to dissipate in only a few minutes

## Slide 8: Spin-Up Tornado Formation

![Two diagrams: a three-panel schematic of surface convergence-zone vortices being stretched upward by a developing cumulus updraft (Wakimoto and Wilson 1988), and a diagram of a horizontal vorticity roll downwind of mountains being tilted vertical by a strong updraft (NOAA, 1999)](media/TornadoDetection/page-08.png)

- Cause 1: small and shallow circulations along surface convergence zone, stretched upward by strong updraft

  **Figure (Wakimoto et al., 1988):** Three-panel time sequence. Panel 1 shows small cumulus clouds and labeled surface vortices (A, B, C) along a convergence line with wind arrows. Panel 2 shows the clouds growing with updrafts (labeled B, C, D) developing over the vortices. Panel 3 shows a large, deep cumulus tower with a vertical arrow and a spiral (tornado) symbol beneath it, indicating a vortex has been stretched upward into a spin-up tornado.

- Cause 2: horizontal vorticity roll downwind of mountains, tilted to vertical when crossed by a strong updraft

  **Figure (NOAA, 1999):** Two side-by-side panels. Left panel shows a 3-D schematic of a horizontal roll vortex (shown as a series of circles/arrows) downwind of a mountain range and a house, driven by "Steeper Wind" / "Southeast Wind" flow, with red arrows indicating updraft. Right panel shows the roll vortex being tilted vertical into a visible funnel/tornado as it is crossed by a strong updraft, red arrows showing vertical flow.

## Slide 9: Doppler Radar Limitations (overview)

![Bullet list slide, no image, introducing the four limitations](media/TornadoDetection/page-09.png)

All Doppler radars have difficulty detecting circulations...

- Too far from radar
  - Beam broadening
  - Overshooting
- Too close to radar
- Blocked from radar view
- Dissipate too quickly

---

## Slide 10: Limitation 1 — Too Far From the Radar (Beam Broadening)

![Diagram of a radar beam cone widening with distance, with a table of beam diameter vs. range](media/TornadoDetection/page-10.png)

- Beam grows too large compared to size of the circulation
  - Large # of slow wind returns outside tornado outweigh small # of fast wind returns within
  - Averaged away/toward velocities too small to represent a threat
- Effective detection range depends on circulation size; effective detection range ≤ 63 statute miles (sm) for the WSR-88D (WSR-88D Operational Support Facility, 1997)
- For very small tornadoes, or radars with larger beam diameters, the range shrinks even shorter

**Figure — Cause 1: Beam Broadening (WSR-88D Operational Support Facility, 1998):** Diagram of a radar beam as a cone with "3 dB angular beamwidth = 0.96°" widening from the radar antenna outward to diameter D at increasing range. Table of example beam diameters: at Range = 60 nmi, Beam Diameter = 1 nmi; at 120 nmi → 2 nmi; at 180 nmi → 3 nmi; at 240 nmi → 4 nmi.

---

## Slide 11: Limitation 1 — Too Far From the Radar (Overshooting)

![Same Volume Coverage Pattern 11 beam-angle diagram as slide 4, annotated to show overshoot distances](media/TornadoDetection/page-11.png)

- Beams rise above low-level circulations because beams travel away from the radar at an angle
- Many NM spin-ups only extend up to about 3,000 ft
- Overshot by center of lowest beam at 40 sm from radar
- Overshot by center of second lowest beam at 29 sm, so spin-ups may only be detectable out to 29 sm (WSR-88D Operational Support Facility, 1997)
- New scan strategy coming
  - Adds 3 new angles at lowest levels
  - Better vertical display of low-level storm structure

**Figure — Cause 2: Overshooting (NWS, 2003):** Same Volume Coverage Pattern 11 beam-angle chart as Slide 4 (height in thousands of feet vs. range in miles for the full set of elevation angles), used here to illustrate how the lowest beams rise above a shallow (~3,000 ft) circulation beyond about 29–40 sm.

---

## Slide 12: Limitation 2 — Too Close to the Radar

![Volume Coverage Pattern 11 diagram annotated with an arrow labeled "Cone of Silence" pointing to the unsampled region directly above the radar](media/TornadoDetection/page-12.png)

- **Doppler radars don't sample the atmosphere directly above them**
- Radars cannot detect circulations that move into the cone of silence
- WSR-88D's cone prevents detection of mid-level circulations within about 11.5 sm of the radar
- Neighboring radar beams overshoot the low levels and the lower portion of the mid levels over both the Albuquerque and Cannon AFB radars

**Figure — Cone of Silence (NWS, 2003):** Volume Coverage Pattern 11 beam diagram (same as Slides 4 and 11) with an arrow labeled "Cone of Silence" pointing to the near-radar, low-range/high-angle region where the beams do not sample.

---

## Slide 13: Limitation 3 — Blocked From View

![Map of New Mexico and surrounding states showing radar coverage circles at 10,000 ft for Albuquerque, Cannon AFB, Holloman AFB, Amarillo, Lubbock, Midland/Odessa, and El Paso radars, with a shaded/hatched region over the mountainous terrain indicating blocked coverage](media/TornadoDetection/page-13.png)

- Mountains can shield low-level circulations from view
- **If not for mountains, we'd see perfect circles of coverage like those in TX**
- Since lower elevation angles intercept more terrain, coverage decreases below 10,000 ft
- Since higher elevation angles intercept less terrain, coverage improves above 10,000 ft
- When implemented, the new scan strategy will slightly improve detection capabilities by increasing sampling of the atmosphere just beyond and above mountain tops

**Figure — Radar Coverage at 10,000 Ft:** Map outline of New Mexico (and edges of neighboring TX/OK/AZ) with labeled radar sites — Santa Fe, Albuquerque, DoD Cannon AFB (Amarillo direction), DoD Holloman AFB, Alamogordo, Las Cruces, El Paso — each with a plotted circular/scalloped coverage boundary at 10,000 ft. The Albuquerque and Cannon AFB coverage circles are irregular/scalloped over the shaded mountainous terrain (reflecting beam blockage), while the Texas-based radars (Amarillo, Lubbock, Midland/Odessa) show cleaner, more circular coverage over flatter terrain.

## Slide 14: Limitation 4 — Dissipate Too Quickly

![Bullet list slide, no image, describing the timing problem between short-lived spin-ups and radar scan/warning cycles](media/TornadoDetection/page-14.png)

- Many spin-ups may last only a few minutes
- Complete atmosphere scan may take 5 or 6 minutes
- Forecaster analysis of images and dissemination of a tornado warning takes extra time
- Even when detected, spin-ups frequently dissipate before a warning can reach people
- New scan strategies will shrink scans to 4.1 minutes

## Slide 15: Example 1 — Spin-Up Below Cumulus Cloud

![Four sequential photographs (Photo 1–4) of a developing funnel/spin-up tornado beneath a cumulus cloud over open grassland, taken from a roadside](media/TornadoDetection/page-15.png)

- March 2003, Torrance County, east of Manzano mtns.
- Not detected by forecasters for 3 reasons
  - Too far: 63 sm from Albuquerque radar and 128 sm from Cannon AFB radar
  - Manzanos blocked the lowest 1½ beams from Albuquerque radar; radar sampled no lower than 6,000 ft above spin-up
  - No rain or thunderstorm activity on this day. Forecasters use the prominent returns from rain, hail and thunderstorm outflow boundaries to identify possible tornado locations.

**Photos 1–4:** Sequence of four roadside photographs of open plains/grassland under a large cumulus cloud. Progressing across the photos, a narrow funnel/condensation column becomes visible descending from the cloud base, consistent with a small non-supercell "spin-up" tornado (landspout) developing beneath a growing cumulus tower, with a road, fence line, and utility pole visible in the foreground of the first photo.

---

## Slide 16: Example 2 — Spin-Up Below Thunderstorm

![Photograph of a clearly visible, well-formed funnel/tornado extending from a low cloud base to the ground over flat, open terrain](media/TornadoDetection/page-16.png)

- May 2003, near Willard in Torrance County, east of Manzanos
- Storms nearly stationary this day
- Forecasters issued a severe thunderstorm warning about 40 minutes before this photo; this spin-up may have **developed along the earlier storm's outflow boundary**
- 59 sm from Albuquerque radar; this tornado may have been big enough to detect
- Manzanos blocked lowest 1½ beams from Albuquerque radar; radar sampled no lower than about 5,000 feet above this spin-up

**Photo:** A single photograph showing a narrow, funnel-shaped tornado condensation column reaching from a low, hazy cloud base down to the ground over flat, open terrain, with what appears to be distant structures/utility poles in the foreground.

## Slide 17: Other Tornado Detection Capabilities

![Bullet list with a graphic of a tipping balance scale labeled "WARN" and "DON'T WARN"](media/TornadoDetection/page-17.png)

- Storm Prediction Center guidance
- Weather models
- Satellites, profilers, soundings, upper-air data, surface obs
- Storm spotters
- News media, law enforcement and public reports
- Research collaboration
  - Testing new scan strategy
  - **Will help confirm sonar's ability to detect tornadoes**

**Graphic:** An old-fashioned two-pan balance scale, tipped so the "WARN" pan is lower/heavier than the "DON'T WARN" pan — illustrating the forecaster's decision trade-off of warning vs. not warning given imperfect detection information.

## Slide 19: Bottom Line

![Bullet list slide, no image, summarizing the talk's conclusions](media/TornadoDetection/page-19.png)

- **WSR-88D's do a good job at what they're designed to do: detect strong mid-level circulations**
- All weather radars have limitations
- Small tornadoes frequently exploit these limitations in New Mexico
- NWS forecasters use all available resources to overcome tornado detection limitations
- Weatherwise media & public can prepare for this dangerous threat and react appropriately when tornadoes strike
