## Background

Tornadoes have been and remain one of the most heavily researched topics within mesoscale meteorology. Yet, understanding of their complex and unique processes from genesis to decay continues to be incomplete. Annually, roughly 1,200 tornadoes occur in the United States, though these numbers may not be wholly representative of the true number given that official tornado records began after 1950, and the advancements in tornado observation in the last few decades (NSSL). Tornadoes have furthermore caused an average of $1.46 billion in damage annually over the past decade, and typically an average of 80 deaths and over 1,500 injuries each year (US Department of Commerce). In 2023, tornadoes resulted in approximately $1.38 billion in damage across the country (Salas 2024) and 83 fatalities across twelve different states (Center for Disaster Philanthropy 2024). It should be noted, however, that fatal tornadoes comprise only about 2% of all tornadoes, according to NOAA (National Weather Service (U.S.) 2017).

### 1.I. Convective Initiation and Storm Mode

Before explicitly discussing the tornadogenesis process, it is necessary to describe the atmospheric conditions necessary to develop tornado-producing storms first. For any thunderstorm to form, three ingredients must be present: moisture, instability, and lifting. To assess moisture, boundary layer (0–2 km above ground level [AGL]) dewpoints are considered. Typically, surface dewpoints greater than 12.8°C will promote stronger thunderstorms, as dewpoints closer to the surface temperature will result in a lower lifted condensation level (LCL) (Haby 2017). Instability is a condition in which air will rise freely on its own due to positive buoyancy, and the magnitude of instability can often assess the strength of a thunderstorm's updraft. For explosive thunderstorm development, ample convective available potential energy (CAPE) is often locked above a weak temperature inversion, where convective inhibition (CIN) is present. This "cap" will allow for greater instability by surface warming and low-level moisture advection without generating convection until the cap is broken. Finally, lift is necessary to raise surface parcels to a point in the atmosphere where they can freely rise without forcing. Lifting mechanisms can come in the form of upslope flow, low pressure systems, or, most commonly in severe convection, frontal boundaries (Ahrens and Henson 2016). The ingredient necessary to organize storms to a state capable of low/mid-level rotation is wind shear, in the form of directional shear (winds change direction with increasing altitude) and speed shear (winds increase in speed with increasing altitude). Numerous studies have examined the influence of wind shear on the rotation of thunderstorms and subsequently tornado production (Browning 1964), (Weisman and Klemp 1982), (Davies-Jones 1984), (Davies-Jones 2015) (Muehr et al. 2024), and it is widely known that cyclonic rotation of a thunderstorm updraft is a product of surface streamwise vorticity (spin) by sufficient speed shear and a veering profile (winds rotating counterclockwise with increasing altitude).

Variations within the magnitudes of shear and instability can lead to different types of storm organizations. A study by Weisman and Klemp (1982) demonstrated that weak shear will produce single-cell thunderstorms (brief, self-contained updraft and downdraft lifecycle), low-to-moderate shear will produce multicells (clusters of storms in different stages of their life cycle behaving as a single unit), and moderate-to-high shear will produce supercells (persistent and severe thunderstorms with a rotating updraft), which is typically the storm mode associated with most tornadoes (Markowski and Richardson 2010). In addition, the study furthermore concluded that differing levels of buoyancy can assist in developing the features present in each storm mode. Further studies have contextualized this relationship, such as Thompson et al. 2012, which found that "environmental differences between the supercells and linear modes were relatively small, indicating that point measures of buoyancy and vertical wind shear alone are not able to clearly discriminate between storm modes in a practical sense." Therefore, this relationship of shear and buoyancy is quite intricate and serves more as a general guide in analyzing storm mode. Further analysis of shear vector and mean wind orientation when storms initialize is necessary to fully forecast and determine storm mode.

### 1.II. Tornadogenesis

Many tornadoes, and an overwhelming majority of strong tornadoes, are produced from the mesocyclone of supercell thunderstorms (Davies-Jones 1984). Davies-Jones 2015 defines supercells as a "a large, quasi-steady, unicellular, propagating storm with a rotating updraft," with cyclonically rotating updraft being defined as the mesocyclone. Unlike any other storm mode, supercells contain two downdrafts, the forward flank downdraft (FFD) on the northeast side of the cell, and the rear flank downdraft (RFD) on the southwest side of the cell. The RFD is generated as dry mid-level air mixes with the moist air within the updraft, subsequently evaporating and cooling, resulting in precipitation wrapping around the updraft. As mentioned previously, the mesocyclone forms aloft because of the updraft tilting storm-relative streamwise vorticity present in the storm's inflow. The study later defines the genesis of a tornado within a mesocyclone as occurring in three phases: 1. Formation of a rotating updraft aloft, 2. Development of a cyclone at the ground, 3. Formation of the tornado. The mesocyclone alone is not sufficient for generating vertical vorticity at the surface (Davies-Jones and Markowski 2013), and thus surface vorticity must be generated by another mechanism. One possible mechanism by which surface vorticity can be generated is through processes associated with the rear flank downdraft (Parker and Dahl 2015). Supercells are unique in that they contain a forward-flank and rear-flank downdraft, as seen in Fig. 1., and the positioning of these downdrafts can assist in building positive vertical vorticity at the surface (Fischer and Dahl 2020). Another possible mechanism is that complex, random perturbations of vorticity (Gaudet et al. 2006; Dahl 2020) develop into a symmetric vortex as these maxima merge (Parker 2023). The vortex column is stabilized by cyclostrophic balance (a force balance between pressure gradient force and centrifugal force), but surface drag within the tornado boundary layer (lowest few meters AGL) disrupts that balance, resulting in an inward directed acceleration of air. The flow direction now transitions from horizontal to upwards (called *corner flow* (Lewellen and Lewellen 2007)), demonstrated in Fig. 2., and parcels entering the column now do not rely on the downdraft mechanism for vertical vorticity (Fischer et al. 2024). Summarized by Fischer et al. 2024, "the development of the tornado boundary layer and corner flow, and thus the tornado, hinges on the development of the intense pressure deficit that results from the axisymmetrization and stretching of the vertical vorticity extrema." In other words, a tornado forms when strong, localized surface low pressure develops, caused by the spinning and stretching of rotating air near the storm's center. A tornado will then dissipate when disruption occurs externally, or the mesocyclone weakens (Marquis et al. 2012).

The aforementioned steps of tornadogenesis are known to be responsible for tornado formation within a supercells, but the process may differ for other storm modes that produce tornadoes. Quasi-Linear Convective System (QLCS) tornadoes, for example, will develop through mesovorticies within the QLCS (Atkins et al. 2004), though through simulations, it was determined that "the primary contribution of an external boundary to QLCS mesovortex genesis was an enhancement of horizontal convergence, rather than the tilting of horizontal baroclinic vorticity associated with the boundary" (Wheatley and Trapp 2008). Often, these low-level mesovorticies develop north of bowing line segments (Weisman and Trapp 2003) independent of mid-level line-end mesovorticies (Weisman 1993). Regardless, tornadogenesis within the mesovortex is still largely due to frictional effects at the surface (Tochimoto and Niino 2022), though the longevity of these tornadoes is often less than that of supercellular tornado due to a lack of persistency of a deep, rotating updrafts as a result of the assorted dynamics within a QLCS cold pool (Marion and Trapp 2021a). It is also worth mentioning that tornadoes originating from storm modes other than supercells are typically weaker, with the ratings of QLCS tornadoes rarely exceeding EF-2 strength (Smith et al. 2012; Marion and Trapp 2021b).

### 1.III. WSR-88Ds

Tornado warnings issued by the NWS Weather Forecasting Offices (WFOs) rely on the detection of an immediate tornado threat (Brotzge and Donner 2013), determined by a conglomerate of evidence suggesting that a storm will produce, is producing, or has produced a tornado. Data from the Weather Surveillance Radar - 1988 Doppler (WSR-88D) network are the primary source providing this information (McCarthy 2002; Simmons and Sutter 2005).¹ The WSR-88D network includes 160 S-band (i.e., 10-cm wavelength) Doppler Radars which are utilized by 122 WFOs across the United States (NCEI 2020). Before discussing the methods in which tornado threats are assessed with radar, however, it is necessary to explain how radars function.

> ¹ Additional sources pertaining to the issuance of a tornado warning are public, spotter, and law enforcement reports. Tornado warnings can receive an additional confirmation update through radar and the aforementioned public reports (Brotzge and Donner 2013).

Radars transmit electromagnetic energy within the microwave spectrum, where the electric and magnetic waves aligned in perpendicular fields oscillate as they travel through air, water, and ice at a speed slightly less than the speed of light. Additionally, the microwave portion of the electromagnetic spectrum are subdivided by wavelength. The most common wavelengths utilized in radars are S-Band (λ = 7.5 to 15 cm), C-Band (λ = 3.75 to 7.5 cm), and X-Band (λ = 2.5 to 3.75 cm) (Stull 2020). Radars operate on the principle of scattering; energy that strikes a target will scatter into many directions, with a fraction of that energy returning to the radar site. The radar will then "listen" for the portion of energy returned in the direction of the radar. There are four main components to radar: the transmitter, an antenna, a receiver, and a display. The transmitter will generate the high frequency signal, while the antenna will send out rapid pulses of energy, rotating in a vertical axis to horizontally scan all directions around the radar. The energy takes only about 2 milliseconds to travel 300 km and back. Meteorological radars can also tilt their antennas in the vertical in order to scan above the horizon. A common practice in radar meteorology is to operate in a volume-scan mode, where the antenna will complete a full 360° scan at one elevation angle, then tilt upwards another one to two degrees and complete another full circle, and different volume coverage patterns (VCPs) can be utilized to sample different environments. The antenna can complete 10-20 different elevation angles and then repeat the cycle in approximately four to six minutes. This allows the radar to collect vertical information in addition to the two-dimensional horizontal scan. Furthermore, tilting a radar in the vertical will prevent the beam from being intercepted by surface-level clutter such as trees or buildings that the radar's beam may scatter upon. Once the returning signal is detected by the receiver and subsequently amplified, it is transmitted to a display device, often in the form of a map containing recognizable surface features like county lines, city names, and state boundaries.

The slant range is the distance along the radar ray from the antenna to the target and is the most fundamental measurement made with radar. The equation for range is given by:

$$r = \frac{c\Delta t}{2}$$

Here, c is the speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s), Δt is the time between two adjacent pulses, or bursts in energy transmission. The pulse repetition time, PRT, is the time between successive pulses, and the pulse repetition frequency is the rate at which successive pulses are transmitted and is the inverse of the pulse period. Transmitting energy using pulses rather than a continuous transmission is crucial to radar fundamentals, as it will determine the maximum range, r_max, that radars can observe targets without pulses arriving in a time interval exceeding that of the radar (called ambiguity). This relationship is given by the following equation:

$$r_{max} < \frac{c}{2(PRF)}$$

This equation introduces the dilemma that decreasing the PRF will increase the maximum range, and vice versa, which is especially prevalent when analyzing velocity, which is a unique feature of Doppler radars. Discovered by Christian J. Doppler, the Doppler Effect states that the shift in frequency caused by moving sources of sound are directly proportional to the speed of the source. Translating this to radar meteorology, given that a radar is stationary, a target moving towards the radar will return in a higher frequency, and vice versa. A Doppler radar will measure the frequency shift between the transmitted signal and the received signal and will determine the speed of the object. Doppler radars require high PRFs (around 1000 Hz) to unambiguously determine Doppler radar velocity, which will in turn decrease the range. A remedy to this dilemma is to transmit two pulses at different PRFs, one high, and one low. Comparing the second trip echoes (scattered signals from targets beyond r_max) from the high PRF scan to the scan with low PRF can allow for the filtration of second trip echoes from the data, allowing for a long-range view in addition to Doppler radial velocity, at the cost of doubling the time required to complete a scan.

The path of a radar beam along Earth's surface is influenced by the shape of the Earth, the strategies of beam emittance, and atmospheric properties. If a radar beam was fired horizontally (a 0° tilt), the effect of Earth's curvature will cause a radar beam to become higher above the Earth's surface. As mentioned earlier, the radar will tilt the antenna at an angle to complete samples in the vertical. Naturally, this will result in the height of the beam increasing as the beam travels further from the radar in addition to the increasing heights from the effect of Earth's curvature. For a beam released at a 1° tilt, beam heights of 10 km will be reached 300 km away from the radar, as observed in Fig. 3. It is furthermore easier to plot the trajectory of a beam as a straight line, but the true nature of a beam's path is also subject to fluctuations in atmospheric density due to variations in temperature and vapor pressure.

The targets that have been alluded to previously are essentially any object that the beam can scatter upon. However, to gain information about the state of convection within the atmosphere, these targets are hydrometeors, which are defined as "any product of condensation or deposition of atmospheric water vapor, whether formed in the free atmosphere or at the earth's surface; also, any water particle blown by the wind from the earth's surface" (AMS). Assuming that these particles consist of dielectric spheres and that they are sufficiently small enough that Rayleigh scattering theory is applicable, the radar reflectivity factor can be derived:

$$Z = \frac{\Sigma_j D_j^6}{V_c}$$

The radar reflectivity factor is "the sum of the sixth power of the diameters of all the raindrops within the contributing region divided by the volume of the contributing region" (Rauber and Nesbitt 2018), and is widely regarded as the most important measurement in radar meteorology. This quantity is expressed in logarithmic units, as several orders of magnitude are possible when sampling the atmosphere. In straightforward terms, the radar reflectivity factor (or simply reflectivity) *allows radars to determine the intensity and location of rainfall in a volume*. This product is crucial in estimating rainfall totals and identifying the structure, intensity, and location of any storm system given a composite of all volumes sampled within the radar's VCP.

The shape a storm assumes based upon its reflectivity is widely utilized when assessing storm mode. Supercells are particularly easy to identify given their unique shape owing to their internal dynamics. The precipitation within the FFD will generate the main part of the radar echo, and particularly high reflectivity (>60 dBZ) can be observed within the core of the FFD due to the presence of often large hail. The RFD, still containing somewhat high reflectivity (30-55 dBZ), will create the hook echo on the southwest side of the storm, and the presence of a sustained hook echo is a prevalent visual indicator that a supercellular storm mode is ongoing (Moller et al. 1994), and the presence of a mesocyclone is likely. Linear storm modes, such as Quasi-Linear Convective Systems (QLCSs) are also often quite identifiable using reflectivity, as seen in Fig. 4. Spanning over 100 km, these are a conglomerate of cells organized in a continuous line and are thus characterized by a frontal line of strong convection, and a trailing region of stratiform precipitation. The precipitation from these storms are typically directed downwind by the strong winds aloft (Moller et al. 1994), with the leading line demonstrating a strong low-level reflectivity as a result of strong convergence and robust vertical updrafts (US Department of Commerce). Therefore, the leading lines of QLCSs will observe reflectivity values between 45-60 dBZ, while the trailing stratiform region will observe reflectivity values > 40 dBZ. It is important to note, however, that the classification of linear storm modes can often be complex, and the morphology of these systems can sometimes vary (Gallus et al. 2008; Duda and Gallus 2010).

Because Doppler radars are capable of detecting motion of the targets they are observing, the motion of precipitation within storms can be determined. It is important to note that the radar will measure radial velocity, meaning that velocity can only be calculated for targets following the direction of the beam within a pulse volume. Therefore, "Doppler radars cannot detect the tangential components of target motion perpendicular to the radar beam" (Rauber and Nesbitt 2018). As a result, the greater the angle of the true target velocity to the radar beam, the less true velocity the radar can sample from the target. This notion of frequency shift will divide motion into two categories; inbound velocity, where the phase shift is negative and velocities will be displayed as a negative value, and outbound velocity, where the phase shift is positive and velocities will be displayed as a positive value. In simpler terms, inbound velocities represent objects moving *towards* the radar, while outbound velocities represent objects moving *away* from the radar, which can be seen in Fig. 5. Therefore, we can diagnose the motion of storms when a Doppler radar has completed its pulse volume scan by determining whether convection is moving towards or away the radar, given that they do not travel perpendicular to the radar beam.

As discussed previously, the "Doppler dilemma" poses a serious challenge to forecasters when balancing an adequate maximum range and maximum unambiguous velocity. However, velocity folding can furthermore complicate the interpretation of radial velocity patterns. Because a phase change greater than π will result in ambiguity sampled in the radial velocity, there is a finite range of motion a Doppler radar can detect. The Nyquist velocity is the maximum unambiguous velocity the radar can measure (v_max) and the range of velocities that the radar can unambiguously measure is defined as the Nyquist interval (-v_max to +v_max). True radial velocities that exceed the maximum unambiguous velocity will be "folded" back into the observable range because the target(s) being observed have a Doppler shift that exceeds +/- π, resulting in a false sense of phase change in the opposite direction. On a display, this will appear as if strong velocities in the inbound (outbound) suddenly shift to the opposite side of the scale as outbound (inbound) velocities. The true radial velocity can be determined by the following equation:

$$v_{r,true} = v_{1,measured} \pm 2n_1 v_{1,max}$$

Where $v_{r,true}$ is the true radial velocity, $v_{1,measured}$ is the measured radial velocity, $n_1$ is the number of folds, and $v_{1,max}$ is the Nyquist velocity. Additionally, the necessity to operate radars at a high PRF to widen the Nyquist interval to avoid folding will create a greater likelihood of range-folded (second trip) echoes due to a small unambiguous range. These unrecoverable velocities have been coded by WSR-88Ds as purple on a display, leading the coining of the term "purple haze" (Torres and Zrnic 2004).

### 1.IV. The Tornadic Vortex Signature

Given that Doppler radar can detect storm motion, the subsequent ability of radar to detect rotation is crucial to identifying tornadoes and storm-scale rotation commonly associated with the mesocyclone prior to tornadogenesis. Given that cyclonic rotation is present within a mesocyclone, the radar will display a 3-10 km couplet of 10-15 ms⁻¹ inbound and outbound velocities perpendicular to the radar beam (Brown and Wood), with the inbounds located left of the beam, and the outbounds located right of the beam. Mesocyclones identified by Doppler radar have and average rotational velocity of 23 ms⁻¹, a core diameter of 5 km, a vertical extent of 8 km, and a duration exceeding an hour (Zrnić et al. 1985). This is demonstrated in Fig. 6, with an example mesocyclone with tangential velocities of 25 ms⁻¹ and a flow radius of 3 km. Additionally, the peak Doppler velocity value is higher (43 ms⁻¹) for stronger mesocyclone than for weaker mesocyclone (29 ms⁻¹) (Brown and Wood 2015), and the flow shape, orientation to radar, asymmetry, and internal velocity structure will all impact the assessment of mesocyclonic intensity when using peak-to-peak shear (Desrochers and Harris 1996). As such, mesocyclone detection algorithms have been developed to identify regions of vertically correlated and symmetrical wind shear indicative of a rotating updraft (Crum and Alberty 1993; Stumpf et al. 1998a). However, a natural consequence of increasing beam height at further ranges will sometimes limit these algorithms specifically designed to separate mesocyclonic rotation from tornadic rotation. Stumpf et al. (1998b) explains that "because vortex size and radar sampling size are related, tornado vortices at very close ranges will be sampled as mesocyclone-like vortices (with velocity peaks separated by more than one radial). At far ranges, mesocyclones resemble [tornado vortices] (with gate-to-gate velocity peak separation)." Additionally, only 3-10% of all mesocyclones produce tornadoes (depending on subjective vortex detection thresholds) (Jones et al. 2004). Therefore, detecting a mesocyclone alone does not necessarily indicate a tornado is imminent and tornado warnings cannot be made explicitly on the presence of a mesocyclone.

When a tornado does form, a maximum in inbound radial velocities azimuthally adjacent to a maximum in outbound radial velocities is typically representative of the radar-based vortex. This feature is known as a tornadic vortex signature (TVS), and it is a crucial indicator to a forecaster that a tornado is likely occurring (Brown et al. 1978a, 2002; Brown and Wood 2012a). For instance, the Joplin tornado, which had received 17 minutes of lead time, had a prevalent TVS associated with a textbook supercell structure as seen in Fig. 7 (Davies 2021). When observing a TVS with Doppler radar, "as the tornado strengthens, the Doppler velocity signature becomes increasingly dominant relative to the mesocyclone signature" (Brown and Wood 2015). The TVS was first discovered when an extreme positive and negative Doppler velocity couplet separated by a 1.0° azimuth was detected by the Severe Storm Laboratory's 10-cm Doppler radar during the Union City, Oklahoma, tornado on May 24, 1973 (Burgess et al. 1975). This signature was first detected at the midlevels of the storm, then exhibited strengthening as it extended deeper. After coinciding the signal on radar with the damage path of the tornado and simulating a Doppler velocity field, Brown et al. 1978b determined that "Doppler velocity peaks of opposite sign are expected to occur at azimuthal locations separated by about one beamwidth regardless of tornado size, strength, or distance from the radar" (Brown and Wood 2012b). As it was unclear whether all tornado-like signatures aloft evolve into tornadoes touching the ground² due to a lack of observations at the time, the signature was labeled as a tornadic vortex signature.

> ² Recent observations using high resolution mobile radars have concluded that tornadoes form from the ground up, and that mid-level TVS's are not necessarily associated with surface-based vortices. (French et al. 2013, 2014, Houser et al. 2015, 2022)

### 1.V. Limitations of the TVS in Warning Issuance

Since the implementation of the WSR-88D network in the 1990s, the TVS has been used operationally by the NWS to identify and warn for tornadoes. Upgrades to the WSR-88D have furthermore improved the identification of the TVS. For example, following the reduction of azimuthal sampling in low elevation scans from 1.0° to 0.5° in 2008, the separation of the TVS velocity peaks have decreased from 1.0°-2.0° adjacent azimuthal sampling intervals (Wood and Brown 1997) to 0.5°-1.0° (Brown et al. 2005). Notable improvements in the detection of tornadoes have subsequently been observed as a result of the WSR-88Ds' ability to detect rotation associated with mesocyclones and the tornado vortex signature (Crum and Alberty 1993; Bieringer and Ray 1996) with the probability of detection (POD) increasing to nearly 75% by 2002. (Brooks 2004). Since then, there has been some fluctuation in the POD as a byproduct of the NWS's motion to reduce the false alarm ratio (FAR) of tornado warnings (Brooks and Correia 2018, p. 20). As of 2023, the current POD sits at 52.4% with a FAR of 69.5% (Brooks 2024). Additionally, the lead time for tornado warnings has improved from ~3 minutes in 1978, prior to the implementation of the WSR-88D's, (Golden and Adams 2000) to ~13 minutes nationwide by 2012 (Brotzge et al. 2013a). The implementation of super-resolution data, which utilizes post-processing to oversample the data at an azimuthal resolution of 0.5°, in 2006 has also helped (Rauber and Nesbitt 2018). However, the lead time since 2012 has only increased by 2 minutes as of 2024 (NSSL 2024), despite additional sampling strategy improvements such as the Supplemental Adaptive Intra-Volume Low Level Scan (SAILS), which resamples the 0.5° elevation angle within every 1.8 to 2.5 minutes (US Department of Commerce). This stagnation in lead time improving can be attributed to the fact that the TVS is not predictive and is thus of limited use to increase lead time of tornado warnings. Furthermore, a tornado may not always exhibit a TVS on radar (Burgess et al. 2002).

The TVS is a degraded representation of the true tornado wind field because the radar's effective beamwidth is typically larger than the tornado's core diameter (Brown et al. 1978a, 2002). This fact implies that the radar-observed wind field is not an accurate depiction of the true intensity of the tornado's winds. TVS detection is dependent upon a variety of factors including 1) the size and strength of the tornado, 2) the distance between the tornado and the radar, which directly impacts the azimuthal distance contributing to the sample volume, 3) the height of the beam above ground level (AGL), and 4) the native resolution (range and azimuthal) of the radar. While these limitations are a natural consequence of the physical constraints of the radar system, they can have serious consequences on the practicality of using radar to detect tornadoes, especially when they are small, and rotation is concentrated near the ground. For a typical WSR-88D having a half-power azimuthal beamwidth of 0.95°, the physical width of the beam expands by nearly 300 m for roughly each 16 km of distance away from the radar (US Department of Commerce). In addition, even at its lowest elevation angle (0.5°), beam heights can reach >1.5 km above the radar at a range of 100 km under normal atmospheric propagation conditions (NOAA 2023). Either or both of these effects may impede the observation of a TVS. Furthermore, the low density distribution of the WSR-88D locations implies that low-level and sufficiently resolved azimuthal data are unavailable in many areas in the U.S., which is displayed in Fig. 8. Maddox et al. (2002) emphasized this problem noting, "Radar data to support the warning mission of the NWS are very limited below 2 km AGL over much of the contiguous United States". This fact justifies the need for supplemental coverage expanding datasets, such as Terminal Doppler Weather Radar (McCutchan et al. 2024). As of 2024, 45 C-Band radars currently comprise the TDWR network across the contiguous United States. These instruments partially compensate for the coverage limitations of the WSR-88D network, and may lead to improved warning issuance at further distances if there is a TDWR nearby (National Weather Service 2017). However, it is still unclear if there is a relationship between warning issuance and TVS detection, and if distance from radar may influence this relationship.

While the physical limitations of radar observations are one factor related to issuing tornado warnings, another consideration is the role that storm mode may play. The probability of tornado formation varies by storm mode. For example, a climatological study by Grams et al. (2012) determined that 69% of tornadoes within their sample of 448 significant tornadoes from January 2000 to December 2008 formed from discrete supercells, while 20% originated from QLCSs, and 11% originated from non-discrete clusters. Similarly, the supercellular storm mode dominated the sample of tornado events within a similar study by Brotzge et al. (2013), while Tessendorf and Trapp (2000) also found an estimated 20% of tornadoes originate from Quasi-Linear Convective Systems. These statistics represent national averages. Storm mode distribution, however, varies both geographically and seasonally. For example, 35%-50% of tornadoes in the Midwest originate from QLCSs (Trapp et al. 2006; Grams et al. 2012a), while 70-80% of tornadoes in the central Plains form from supercells (Grams et al. 2012). In terms of seasonality, significant tornadoes occur most frequently with discrete/supercellular storm modes in the Great Plains during the spring and summer (Grams et al. 2012a) while supercellular tornadoes are most common across the southeastern U.S during the later winter and early spring months. Another challenge to issuing tornado warnings is an uneven distribution of annual tornadoes across the U.S. As a result, "offices that have the most tornadoes typically perform the best in terms of warning performance" (National Weather Service (U.S.) 2017).

Differences in TVS detection across the spectrum of storm modes has not been explicitly investigated but may impact the subsequent performances of different WFOs in warning issuance and lead time, particularly given the known challenges in warning non-supercellular storm modes. QLCS tornadoes may have less distinct TVSs due to shallower rotation present in shearing instability dominant cases (Goodnight et al. 2022), and non-supercellular tornadoes may conventionally be weaker and more short-lived than mesocyclonic tornadoes spawned by supercells, making them difficult to warn in advance (Wakimoto and Wilson 1989). Additionally, it has been discussed that the radar parameters used to assess tornado intensity within mesocyclones in the supercellular mode, such as storm maximum rotational speed, frequency, and period, or mesocyclone depth, are not applicable in assessing the strength of a QLCS tornado (Gibbs and Bowers 2019). This disparity is further reflected in statistics of warning quality, where the POD of tornadoes originating from supercells ranges from 80-88% and an average lead time of 13-15 min, whereas tornadoes spawned by nonsupercellular storms have a POD between 44-49% and an average lead time of 9-10 min (Brotzge et al. 2013a). Within nonsupercelluar storm modes, QLCS tornadoes were found to also have dramatically lower POD values compared to supercells (Anderson-Frey and Brooks 2021). However, it is important to differentiate that POD does not necessarily reflect the detection of TVSs nor does it imply anything about the quality of the lead time. It simply notes the issuance of a warning³.

Motivated by the complex physical conditions that contribute to tornado warning issuance and lead time including a tornado's distance from the radar, TVS occurrence and detection, and parent storm mode, the objective of this study is to quantify these relationships for a relatively small geographic area that includes portions of Ohio, West Virginia, Indiana and Kentucky, and compare these relationships to what is found for the Norman, OK WFO. Specifically, this study intends to answer the following questions: (i) Are TVSs better detected, and warnings more efficiently issued (i.e. higher rates of warning issuance and lead time) in close proximity to a WSR-88D radar versus farther away? (ii) Does parent storm mode of tornadoes affect radar-based TVS detection, warning issuance or warning timeliness, and how do these results vary with distance from a WSR-88D? (iii) Are storms containing detectable TVSs at tornadogenesis time warned more accurately than storms without detectable TVSs? It follows that the data and methodology are presented in section 2, followed by results and analysis in section 3, then the results are discussed in section 4 followed by overarching conclusions in section 5.

> ³ "Warning issuance" is utilized in this study rather than POD, as warnings issued after tornadogenesis are considered an independent category, which is not represented by POD metrics.

## Data and methods

In order to answer the questions presented above, tornado reports including time, date, and latitude-longitude locations of tornadogenesis points were obtained from the SPC's OneTor database and WSR-88D level-2 radar data. The dataset was comprised of observations from four WFOs including: Wilmington OH (ILN), Charleston, WV (RLX), Indianapolis, IN (IND), and Norman, OK (OUN), and their corresponding radars (KILN, KRLX, KIND and KTLX)⁴. ILN, RLX, and IND were selected based upon the known presence of "radar holes" between adjacent radar sites in this region, author familiarity of recent regional tornado events that were both warned and unwarned, a known variety of parent storm modes, and variable frequencies of tornadoes and tornado warning issuance for the different WFOs. OUN was included along with observations from the nearby KTLX radar to provide a source of comparison to a WFO familiar with frequent tornadoes, high warnings issuance rates (i.e. a WFO where it is assumed that forecasters are highly experienced in issuing tornado warnings), and a different geographical region. Tornado reports from OneTor were classified as "near" the radar, (i.e., ≤ 50 km from the radar), and "far" from the radar, (i.e., 50 – 160 km from the radar). The beam height of the lowest elevation angle (0.5°) at a range of 50 km from the radar is ~1.9 km AGL while it is almost 3 km AGL at a range of 160 km.

> ⁴ Note that WFOs do not carry the "K" in this paper while radars are differentiated from WFOs by keeping the leading "K".

Due to the larger area present in a 50-160 km radius than a 50 km radius, "near" datasets were from 2009-2022, while "far" datasets were from 2012-2022. Because of the considerable variation in the number of tornadoes observed and warnings issued in a given calendar year between the different WFOs (e.g., OUN typically issues 251 warnings each year, while RLX issues 33)⁵, consistency in the number of tornado reports was sacrificed for consistency in timeframe, with the intent to obtain a statistically significant number of tornadoes for each WFO. In a few cases, some observed tornadoes within the 160 km range of the radar were not in the jurisdiction of the corresponding WFO. These tornado reports were discarded from the dataset. In addition, tornadoes that occurred beyond the WFO's County Warning Area (CWA) were also excluded, even if they were within the 160 km range. In total, 522 tornadoes (216 near, 306 far) were included over all four WFOs (Table 1). Answering questions i. and iii. required a consistent definition of a TVS⁶. Here, a TVS was classified by a differential velocity (ΔV) ≥ 30 m s⁻¹ having no more than 4 radar volume pixels or a ground based distance ≤ 2.5 km between the maximum inbound and outbound velocities, following Bentley et al. (2021) but utilizing ΔV instead of $V_{rot}$. Tornado report locations and times from the SPC's OneTor Database were compared with the corresponding level 2 NEXRAD data, and analyzed using the Gibson Ridge Level 2 Analyst software (Gibson 2005). To investigate how TVS detection varies with distance from radar and storm mode, each tornado report was characterized as having either a "detectable" or "undetectable" TVS based upon whether the above criteria were met at the time of the tornado report (detectable) or if one or more were missing (undetectable). Radar data at the 0.5 degree PPI radar sample time closest to the genesis time indicated from the SPC OneTor tornado report, or up to two volume scans prior (to account for temporal error in the storm report), were analyzed to determine the presence or absence of a TVS as defined above. In a few cases, TVSs were categorized as "obstructed" due to their proximity to the "cone of silence" or "purple haze". To investigate rates of warning issuance, each tornado report was also categorized as "warned," "late," or "unwarned", based upon whether the tornado report occurred before (warned) or after (late) a tornado warning was issued or if there was no tornado warning issued within 10 minutes of the report time (unwarned). For warned storms, lead time was first calculated precisely (within 1 minute) then assigned to a binned category by intervals of 5 minutes: > 15 min, 11-15 min, 6-10 min, 0-5 min.

> ⁶ Tornadic Debris Signatures (TDSs) were not utilized in any capacity for this study. The dataset used included tornadoes that occurred prior to 2012 which could introduce inconsistency as WSR-88Ds were not outfitted with Dual-Polarization capabilities, and subsequently, the correlation coefficient product was not available. Furthermore TDS detection is not consistent from tornado to tornado as they are a function of debris availability, height of the radar beam, and strength of the tornado.

To answer question ii., each tornado report was assigned a parent storm mode, based upon known reflectivity and velocity structures and appearances. Options for parent storm type included "Supercell," "QLCS," or "Other." The parent storm mode was identified as a supercell if the parent storm had a mid-level mesocyclone that was identifiable in the radial velocity for at least 10 minutes before the tornado report began. The storm was not required to be isolated so long as the persistent mesocyclone was present. Reflectivity features commonly associated with supercells such as a hook echo, forward flank precipitation region, and/or a hail core were used to aid identification. However, these features were not required for a storm to be characterized as a supercell. A parent storm was characterized as a QLCS if it exhibited a linear convective structure on radar, either with continuous or discrete but linearly adjacent, embedded convective elements (Rauber and Nesbitt 2018). Typically, the convective complex contained a trailing stratiform region of precipitation behind the leading convective line, though this was not a requirement to classify a storm mode as a QLCS. Tornado reports originating from QLCSs often appeared as a "cusp" in the reflectivity along the leading edge, an important distinction in comparison to that of the trailing rear-right location (in a storm-relative sense) of a supercell's hook echo on radar. A precursory radial velocity couplet was sometimes present within these cusps prior to tornadogenesis, but this was not required. A QLCS storm mode was similarly required to maintain the described reflectivity structure for a 10-minute period prior to the tornado report. All tornadoes originating from other storm modes, such as narrow cold-frontal rainbands (NCFR), multicells, convective clusters, etc., were classified as "Other." There were no tropical cyclone tornadoes in this sample.

There are several limitations with the methodology used for this study that must be acknowledged. First, the locations and timing of tornadogenesis are only as reliable as the SPC's OneTor Database reports, which are based on the NCEI storm events database. It is noted that the exact time and start coordinates may not be entirely accurate, particularly for weak and short-lived tornadoes (NCEI). Start time errors will impact the lead time identified herein. Unfortunately, there is no good way to systematically address this limitation. Owing to the known differences in timing of TVS onset between the ground and higher elevations, and the fact that not all tornadoes have a TDS, no attempts were made to try to reconcile OneTor genesis times using either the radar observations of TVSs or TDSs. Second, tornadoes develop over timescales of seconds, while radar scans, depending on the VCP, take 4-5 minutes to complete, with 0.5° revisits every 2 minutes when using SAILS. This timing discrepancy means the exact start of a tornado is rarely captured by radar, potentially resulting in rotation signatures that do not accurately reflect the tornado's radar-based genesis time. Therefore, an approximation using the closest available radar scan to the reported time of genesis is necessary. Third, owing to naturally occurring variations in the geographic frequency of tornadoes, the statistical robustness of each WFO's sample size varies. For example, RLX had a total of 41 tornadoes while ILN had 151. Therefore, the statistical conclusions derived from RLX should be interpreted with caution. Fourth, "near" datasets include tornado reports from before the 2012 implementation of dual-polarization radar. This may result in inconsistencies in warning issuance data, as WFOs could not rely on correlation coefficient data to identify tornadic debris. Consequently, tornadoes reported after the adoption of dual-polarization radar might have received higher rates of warning issuance due to enhanced detection capabilities, potentially affecting comparative analyses of warning strategies over time. However, this limitation is systemic to the full dataset at all ranges and WFOs, and should not likely result in meaningful bias when answering the questions posed herein. Fifth, as mentioned, the azimuthal width of a radar pixel increases significantly with distance from the radar, resulting in larger sampled areas farther out. Consequently, a maximum distance of 2.5 km was set between inbound and outbound velocities to capture rotational features. However, this relatively large distance may also overestimate the counts of tornadoes with a detectable TVS, as it includes a broader area that could encompass non-tornadic vortices. We acknowledge that these limitations will affect the accuracy of certain aspects of this study. There is unfortunately no other option to mitigate them. It is therefore important to be mindful of these limitations when interpreting certain results.

Dr. Jana Houser and I were able to conclude that there exists a strong relationship between the detection of TVSs with distance, storm mode and the issuance of tornado warnings such that TVSs are more likely to be detected and warned when closer to the radar, and when associated with supercells. Warning issuance/lead time was generally related to the parent storm mode but was not a function of distance. However, these latter relationships are inconsistent between WFOs. We believe that differences in geographic frequency of tornadoes and distributions of parent storm mode are considerations that should be acknowledged within a forecasting regime by all WFOs tasked with tornado warning operations. Our hopes are that this study successfully demonstrates the value that WSR-88D radar observations contribute to the warning-decision making process, and we furthermore hope this work brings awareness to the multitude of challenges that WFOs must navigate when making critical decisions about the issuance of a tornado warning in the interest of protecting life and property.
